# Pattern Executive Summary Gold Standard Guide

Use this guide when the goal is a two-page executive summary for market
research, IC memos, diligence, competitive assessments, or strategy work.

## Decision Standard

The executive summary is the shortest decision-grade version of the argument.
It is not a preface, teaser, or table of contents.

A senior reader who reads only the summary must understand:

1. What company, category, or opportunity is being evaluated.
2. What the product does and whether differentiation is structural.
3. What market dynamic makes the opportunity attractive or unattractive.
4. How the business model creates revenue, margin, and scale.
5. What must be true for the thesis to work.
6. What open questions could change the answer.

## Canonical Two-Page Spine

Use this order:

1. Company Overview
2. Product Offering
3. Market Dynamic
4. Business Model
5. Thesis: What You Need To Believe
6. Open Questions

## Section Standards

| Section | What it must answer | Common failure |
|---|---|---|
| Company Overview | What is this business/category and why does it matter? | Generic company description with no decision relevance |
| Product Offering | What platform/functionality exists and what differentiation is structural vs. transient? | Feature list with no workflow or differentiation test |
| Market Dynamic | What forces make this market attractive or unattractive now? | "Large and growing" without mechanism |
| Business Model | How does value translate into revenue, margin, retention, and scalability? | Revenue model stated without economics |
| Thesis | What must be true to believe the opportunity is attractive? | Positives listed as facts rather than beliefs |
| Open Questions | Which diligence gaps could change the recommendation? | Laundry list of low-impact questions |

## Workflow

### 1. Extract Before Writing

Complete this table before drafting:

| Section | Required extraction |
|---|---|
| Company Overview | Description, customers, scale, stage/status, decision relevance |
| Product Offering | Workflows, functionality, users, integrations, differentiation |
| Market Dynamic | Size/growth, timing, demand drivers, competition, headwinds |
| Business Model | Revenue model, pricing, margins, retention, sales motion, scalability |
| Thesis | 3-5 falsifiable beliefs required for the opportunity to work |
| Open Questions | Gaps that could change proceed/reprice/pass posture |

### 2. Classify Differentiation

Product differentiation must be labeled:

| Label | Definition |
|---|---|
| Structural | Hard to copy because of data, workflow embed, distribution, scale, switching costs, regulation, or network effects |
| Transient | Copyable feature, UI, implementation, integration, messaging, or service lead |
| Mixed | Durable elements exist but execution or current market position still matters |
| Unproven | Source evidence does not support a durability claim |

Required sentence:

> Differentiation assessment: [structural/transient/mixed/unproven] because
> [specific evidence].

### 3. Write Page 1

Page 1 should orient the reader:

- Company Overview: 120-170 words.
- Product Offering: 150-220 words.
- Market Dynamic: 180-260 words.

### 4. Write Page 2

Page 2 should explain economics, beliefs, and gaps:

- Business Model: 150-220 words.
- Thesis: 3-5 bullets, each a Need-to-Believe.
- Open Questions: 4-7 questions, each tied to decision impact.

## Quality Gates

- The summary can stand alone without the full report.
- It preserves the actual recommendation posture.
- Every number is sourced or labeled as estimate/hypothesis.
- Product differentiation is classified.
- Thesis bullets are beliefs, not facts.
- Open questions are decision-changing.
- No section repeats another section's job.
- No generic "large market / strong product / experienced team" language.

## Paste-Ready Prompt

```text
Write a two-page executive summary for [source document / company / market].

Use the canonical six-section spine: Company Overview, Product Offering, Market
Dynamic, Business Model, Thesis: What You Need To Believe, and Open Questions.
Classify product differentiation as structural, transient, mixed, or unproven.
Label facts, estimates, hypotheses, and gaps. Keep the summary decision-grade:
it should stand alone for a senior reader and preserve what could change the
recommendation.
```
