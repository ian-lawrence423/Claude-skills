# Executive Summary Writer

Use this skill to turn completed analytical work into a two-page executive
summary. The output is not a table of contents, a teaser, or a softened version
of the memo. It is the shortest decision-grade version of the argument.

Read this file before writing an executive summary.

---

## Core Rule

The default executive summary is a **two-page, six-section summary** in this exact
order:

1. Company Overview
2. Product Offering
3. Market Dynamic
4. Business Model
5. Thesis: What You Need To Believe
6. Open Questions

Do not replace this with a generic "governing thesis / three reasons / risk /
recommendation" structure unless the user explicitly asks for a one-page memo.
For IC memos and market research reports, this six-section spine is canonical.

---

## What The Summary Must Do

A senior reader who reads only the executive summary must understand:

1. What the company is and why it matters
2. What the product does and whether differentiation is structural or transient
3. What market forces create or constrain the opportunity
4. How the business model converts product value into revenue, margin, and scale
5. What must be true for the thesis to work
6. Which open questions could change the conclusion

If any of those six answers are missing, the summary is incomplete.

---

## Step 1: Identify The Source Mode

Use the same six-section spine in every mode. Change the lens, not the structure.

| Mode | Use when | Primary lens |
|---|---|---|
| **IC memo** | Summarizing an investment memo, deal write-up, or diligence package | Should we proceed, reprice, pause, or pass? |
| **Market research** | Summarizing a market, category, competitor set, or strategic opportunity | Is this market attractive and strategically actionable? |
| **Strategy memo** | Summarizing a strategic option, product thesis, or operating initiative | What decision should leadership make now? |
| **Diligence update** | Summarizing work-in-progress diligence | What have we learned, what remains unknown, and what changes the answer? |

If the source is a pure market scan with no target company, keep the section title
`Company Overview` and use it to define the company archetype, target segment, or
platform category under review. Do not drop the section.

---

## Step 2: Extract Before Writing

Do not draft until the following extraction table is complete.

| Section | Extract from source | Evidence standard |
|---|---|---|
| Company Overview | Business description, customers, scale, ownership/status, decision relevance | Named facts where available; flag missing scale data |
| Product Offering | Platform description, core workflows, buyer/user, functionality, integrations, differentiation | Label differentiation as structural, transient, mixed, or unproven |
| Market Dynamic | Market size/growth, demand drivers, competitive structure, timing, headwinds/tailwinds | Source every figure; separate facts from estimates |
| Business Model | Revenue model, pricing, gross margin, retention, sales motion, expansion, scalability | Use actual metrics if available; otherwise mark as gap |
| Thesis | Three to five beliefs required for the opportunity to work | Each belief must be testable and falsifiable |
| Open Questions | Diligence gaps, risk questions, evidence needed, decision impact | Only include questions that could change the conclusion |

Use evidence tags when evidence quality varies:
- `[F]` Fact: directly sourced, named, and reliable
- `[E]` Estimate: derived or triangulated with explicit assumptions
- `[H]` Hypothesis: plausible but not yet tested
- `[GAP]` Missing evidence required to confirm or reject the thesis

---

## Step 3: Write The Two-Page Summary

### Page 1 - Orientation And Market Logic

Page 1 should answer: **What is this company, what does it sell, and why does the
market context matter now?**

#### 1. Company Overview

Target length: 120-170 words.

Required content:
- What the company does in one direct sentence
- Who it serves: customer segment, buyer, and end user when different
- Current scale or traction: revenue, ARR, GMV, customers, geographies, or stage
- Ownership/status: private, public, sponsor-backed, founder-owned, target, partner, or competitor
- Why it matters for the decision at hand

Output standard:
- Factual orientation first, interpretation second
- No market claims unless they explain why the company matters
- If scale is unknown, state the missing metric explicitly rather than implying traction

#### 2. Product Offering

Target length: 150-220 words.

Required content:
- Description of the platform, product, or service
- Core functionality and workflows supported
- Buyer, user, and operational job-to-be-done
- Where the product sits in the customer workflow or technology stack
- Differentiation assessment: **structural**, **transient**, **mixed**, or **unproven**

Use this test for differentiation:

| Differentiation type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Structural** | Advantage is hard to copy because it is embedded in data, workflow, distribution, scale, switching costs, regulatory position, or network effects | Proprietary data asset, system-of-record workflow, dense partner network, regulated license base |
| **Transient** | Advantage can erode as competitors copy features, pricing, messaging, integrations, or service model | UI lead, faster implementation, broader current feature set, channel promotion |
| **Mixed** | Some durable elements exist, but the current advantage still depends on execution or temporary market position | Workflow depth plus copyable features; data asset without proven exclusivity |
| **Unproven** | The source document does not provide enough evidence to classify the advantage | Vendor claims without customer proof, no churn data, no competitor benchmarks |

Required sentence:
`Differentiation assessment: [structural/transient/mixed/unproven] because [specific reason].`

#### 3. Market Dynamic

Target length: 180-260 words.

Required content:
- Market structure: expanding, consolidating, mature, fragmented, regulated, or winner-take-most
- Demand drivers: what customer pain or external force is increasing urgency
- Competitive intensity: named competitors or competitor types, not generic "competitive market"
- Timing: why now, why not earlier, and what could close the window
- Tailwinds and headwinds that affect the investment or strategic decision

Output standard:
- State the market dynamic as a conclusion, not a topic
- Use figures only when sourced or clearly labeled as estimates
- Explain the implication for the company, not just the market

---

### Page 2 - Economics, Beliefs, And Gaps

Page 2 should answer: **How does this become an attractive business or decision,
what must be true, and what still needs to be proven?**

#### 4. Business Model

Target length: 150-220 words.

Required content:
- Revenue model: subscription, usage, transaction, services, license, marketplace, take-rate, or hybrid
- Pricing logic: who pays, what value metric drives price, and whether pricing scales with customer value
- Revenue quality: recurring, re-occurring, transactional, project-based, concentrated, or diversified
- Unit economics: gross margin, CAC/payback, retention, expansion, contribution margin, or proxies
- Scalability: what improves or degrades with scale

Output standard:
- Separate product value from economic capture
- Do not call the business model attractive without evidence from margin, retention, pricing power, or expansion
- If financial data is missing, state the specific metric needed

#### 5. Thesis: What You Need To Believe

Target length: 220-320 words.

Write three to five belief statements. Each belief must be binary enough that
diligence can confirm or reject it.

Required format:

```markdown
- **Believe [specific proposition].** Current evidence: [fact/estimate/hypothesis].
  This matters because [implication]. Falsified if [specific trigger].
```

IC memo belief categories:
- Company quality: the business has durable customer value and defensible position
- Sector timing: the market window is attractive now
- Investment attractiveness: entry price, value creation, and downside case can support the required return
- Product/business model quality: the offering converts usage into durable revenue and margin
- Exit or strategic relevance: the asset will matter to logical buyers or public-market investors

Market research belief categories:
- Customer urgency: the problem is painful and budgeted
- Market expansion: growth is real and not just vendor narrative
- Competitive white space: the opportunity is not already foreclosed by incumbents
- Economics: the category can support attractive revenue quality and margins
- Strategic fit: the market creates an actionable opportunity for the company or investor

Output standard:
- Do not write "we believe" without saying what must be true
- Do not list generic thesis pillars like "large market" or "strong product"
- Every belief must carry evidence quality and a falsification trigger

#### 6. Open Questions

Target length: 100-170 words.

Include three to five questions only. Prioritize by decision impact.

Required format:

```markdown
1. **[Question]** Decision impact: [what changes if the answer is adverse].
   Evidence needed: [source, analysis, customer call, dataset, management request].
```

Open questions must be true decision questions, not workplan tasks.

Good open questions:
- "Is retention driven by product value or switching friction?"
- "Can gross margin expand after implementation services normalize?"
- "Does the buyer have budget authority or only workflow pain?"
- "Is the apparent product differentiation structural or a temporary feature lead?"

Bad open questions:
- "Conduct more diligence"
- "Review the market"
- "Talk to customers"
- "Confirm the thesis"

## Format Variants

The two-page summary is the default. Other formats are compressed derivatives of
the same six-section spine.

### One-Page Summary

Use only when the user explicitly asks for one page.

Rules:
- Keep all six sections
- Compress each section to one short paragraph or one tight table row
- Thesis may use three beliefs maximum
- Open Questions may use three questions maximum
- Target length: 500-700 words

### Deck Opening

Use for a deck only when pattern-investment-pptx or another deck skill owns the
visual format.

Rules:
- Slide 1: Company Overview, Product Offering, Market Dynamic
- Slide 2: Business Model, Thesis, Open Questions
- Titles must be conclusions, not labels
- Bullets must be assertions with evidence, not topics

### Briefing Paragraph

Use for email, Slack, or verbal prep.

Rules:
- Compress the six sections into one paragraph of 175-225 words
- Preserve Product Offering and Thesis
- Include one open question at the end

## Section-Level Anti-Patterns

### Company Overview

Do not write:
- "The company is a leading provider..."
- "The company operates in a large and growing market..."
- "The company has significant traction..." without the metric

Write:
- "[Company] sells [product] to [buyer/customer] to solve [workflow/problem]. It is
  relevant to this decision because [specific link to thesis]."

### Product Offering

Do not confuse:
- Feature breadth with differentiation
- Better UI with structural advantage
- Services quality with product moat
- Customer logos with product proof

Always classify differentiation explicitly.

### Market Dynamic

Do not write a neutral market overview. State the market conclusion:
- "The market is expanding but not yet winner-take-most..."
- "The market is consolidating around workflow depth and proprietary data..."
- "Customer demand is real, but budget ownership remains fragmented..."

### Business Model

Do not describe the product again. Explain economic capture:
- Who pays
- Why they pay
- Whether revenue repeats
- Whether margin improves with scale
- Which metric proves quality

### Thesis

Do not write vague investment-theory phrases. Use belief statements:
- "You need to believe that..."
- "This is falsified if..."

### Open Questions

Do not include diligence chores. Include only questions whose answers could change
the recommendation, valuation, prioritization, or scope.

## Quality Gates

An executive summary is ready when every gate passes.

**Structure**
- [ ] All six sections are present in the canonical order
- [ ] The output fits two pages unless the user requested a different length
- [ ] Product Offering includes a differentiation classification
- [ ] Thesis is written as three to five belief statements
- [ ] Open Questions includes only decision-changing questions

**Evidence discipline**
- [ ] Every material market, financial, or competitive claim has a source or evidence tag
- [ ] Estimates are labeled and assumptions are explicit
- [ ] Hypotheses are not presented as facts
- [ ] Missing metrics are marked as gaps, not hidden

**Executive readability**
- [ ] The first paragraph explains what the company is and why it matters
- [ ] The market section explains implication for the company or decision
- [ ] The business model section explains economic capture, not product features
- [ ] The thesis section makes the required beliefs testable
- [ ] The summary can be understood without reading the full memo

**Prose standards**
- [ ] No "leading provider," "significant," "robust," "best-in-class," or other hollow intensifiers
- [ ] No table-of-contents language: "This report examines..."
- [ ] No false balance: "opportunities and risks" without a conclusion
- [ ] Active voice throughout
- [ ] Numbers use concise formatting: `$2.3B`, `34%`, `4.2x`, not prose substitutes
