# Source Validation Frameworks

Comprehensive guide to evaluating source credibility and data quality using professional academic and industry standards. Apply these frameworks systematically to ensure research integrity.

---

## CRAAP Test Framework

The CRAAP Test is a widely-used academic and professional standard for evaluating source credibility. Developed by librarians at California State University, it's adopted across business schools and professional research.

### Overview

**CRAAP Acronym:**
- **C**urrency
- **R**elevance
- **A**uthority
- **A**ccuracy
- **P**urpose

**Application:** Score each dimension 1-5, with 20-25 total indicating excellent source, 15-19 acceptable, <15 requiring caution or rejection.

---

### Currency: Timeliness of Information

**Definition:** When was the information published or last updated? Is it current enough for your research needs?

**Evaluation Questions:**

✓ When was the information published or posted?
✓ Has the information been revised or updated recently?
✓ Does your research topic require current information, or will older sources work?
✓ Are the links functional (for web sources)?
✓ Is there evidence of regular maintenance and updates?

**Scoring Criteria:**

| Score | Currency Assessment |
|-------|---------------------|
| 5 | Published within past 12 months; actively maintained; perfect timing for research needs |
| 4 | Published 1-2 years ago; recent enough for topic; evidence of occasional updates |
| 3 | Published 2-3 years ago; acceptable for slow-changing topics; some dated elements |
| 2 | Published 3-5 years ago; aging but usable; significant context required |
| 1 | Published >5 years ago; outdated for most business research; historical value only |

**Industry-Specific Standards:**

- **Technology/Digital:** Maximum 1-2 years old
- **Healthcare/Pharma:** Maximum 3 years (regulations), 5 years (drug development)
- **Consumer goods:** Maximum 2-3 years
- **Heavy industry/Manufacturing:** Maximum 5 years
- **Economic fundamentals:** Maximum 10 years acceptable for context

**Red Flags:**
- No publication date listed
- Broken links or outdated references
- Data predates recent major market shifts (e.g., COVID-19, major regulation changes)
- "Last updated" is many years ago

**Best Practices:**
- Document publication date in bibliography
- Note if using older source for historical context
- Apply documented growth rates to update old market sizes
- When recent data unavailable, explicitly state limitation

---

### Relevance: Importance to Research Needs

**Definition:** Does the information relate directly to your research question? Is it appropriate for your intended audience?

**Evaluation Questions:**

✓ Does the information directly answer your research question?
✓ Is it appropriate for your audience (e.g., client sophistication level)?
✓ Is the geographic coverage appropriate (US vs. Global vs. Specific region)?
✓ Is the industry coverage specific enough or too broad?
✓ Have you examined a variety of sources before selecting this one?
✓ Would you be comfortable citing this source in a client presentation?

**Scoring Criteria:**

| Score | Relevance Assessment |
|-------|---------------------|
| 5 | Directly addresses research question; perfect geographic and industry fit; appropriate detail level |
| 4 | Highly relevant with minor tangents; good fit but some extrapolation needed |
| 3 | Moderately relevant; requires significant context or adjustment to apply |
| 2 | Tangentially relevant; usable for background but not core analysis |
| 1 | Minimally relevant; generic content not specific to research needs |

**Common Relevance Issues:**

**Geographic mismatch:**
- Source: Global data
- Need: US-specific
- Solution: Check if source provides regional breakouts

**Industry definition mismatch:**
- Source: "Food and Beverage"
- Need: "Plant-based protein"
- Solution: Calculate subset if methodology allows

**Audience mismatch:**
- Source: Academic journal (highly technical)
- Need: Executive summary (business-focused)
- Solution: Extract and translate key findings

**Red Flags:**
- Source addresses different question than yours
- Industry categorization doesn't match target market
- B2C data when B2B needed (or vice versa)
- Consumer-facing content when need trade/professional data

**Best Practices:**
- Verify geographic coverage matches analysis needs
- Check industry classification codes (NAICS/SIC) alignment
- Assess appropriate level of detail (not too broad, not too narrow)
- Confirm target audience matches source audience

### Authority: Source of Information

**Definition:** Who is the author/publisher? What are their credentials? Is this source reputable?

**Evaluation Questions:**

✓ Who is the author/creator/publisher?
✓ What are their credentials or organizational affiliations?
✓ Are they qualified to write on this topic?
✓ Is there contact information (indicating willingness to be accountable)?
✓ Does the URL reveal anything about the source (.gov, .edu, .com)?
✓ Has the source been cited by others (forward citation check)?
✓ Is this a well-known, respected organization in the field?

**Scoring Criteria:**

| Score | Authority Assessment |
|-------|---------------------|
| 5 | Tier 1 source: Government agency, peer-reviewed journal, established research org |
| 4 | Tier 2 source: Industry association, known consulting firm, respected think tank |
| 3 | Tier 3 source: Company white paper, trade publication, recognized expert blog |
| 2 | Questionable authority: Unknown organization, unclear credentials, weak reputation |
| 1 | No authority: Anonymous, no credentials, disreputable source |

**Authority Hierarchy by Source Type:**

**Government/Intergovernmental (Highest Authority):**
- US Census Bureau, BLS, Eurostat
- World Bank, IMF, OECD, UN agencies
- National statistical offices
- Regulatory agencies (FDA, EPA, EIA)

**Academic (Very High Authority):**
- Peer-reviewed journals (check impact factor)
- University research centers
- Academic book publishers (Oxford, Cambridge, MIT Press)
- Researchers with established publication records

**Professional Organizations (High Authority):**
- Industry trade associations
- Professional societies (IEEE, AMA, etc.)
- Standards bodies (ISO, ANSI)

**Commercial Research (Moderate-High Authority):**
- Established market research firms (Nielsen, Gartner, Forrester, IDC)
- Top consulting firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Big 4)
- Bloomberg, Reuters (for financial data)

**Company Sources (Moderate Authority, Bias Risk):**
- Public company SEC filings (high factual authority)
- Company investor relations (moderate authority, optimistic bias)
- Company blogs/white papers (lower authority, marketing bias)

**Media (Varies Widely):**
- Tier 1 media: WSJ, FT, Economist, Bloomberg News (fact-checked)
- Tier 2 media: Industry trade publications
- Tier 3 media: Forbes contributors, Medium (no editorial oversight)

**Red Flags:**
- No author listed (anonymous content)
- Author credentials not provided or unrelated to topic
- Organization unknown or recently established
- No "About Us" or contact information
- Self-published without peer review
- Credential exaggeration (fake PhDs, fabricated affiliations)

**Verification Steps:**
1. Google author name + credentials
2. Check organization "About" page and history
3. Verify claimed affiliations (university, company)
4. Check citation count (Google Scholar)
5. Look for conflicts of interest disclosures

### Accuracy: Reliability and Correctness

**Definition:** Is the information supported by evidence? Can you verify it with other sources? Is methodology sound?

**Evaluation Questions:**

✓ Is the information supported by evidence?
✓ Has the information been peer-reviewed or fact-checked?
✓ Can you verify claims with other sources?
✓ Does the language/tone seem objective and professional?
✓ Are there obvious errors (spelling, grammar, broken links)?
✓ Is the methodology clearly explained?
✓ Are limitations acknowledged?
✓ Are sources cited properly?

**Scoring Criteria:**

| Score | Accuracy Assessment |
|-------|---------------------|
| 5 | All claims substantiated; methodology transparent; limitations acknowledged; cross-validated |
| 4 | Most claims substantiated; good methodology; minor limitations in transparency |
| 3 | Key claims substantiated; methodology adequate but not detailed; some verifiability issues |
| 2 | Limited substantiation; unclear methodology; difficulty verifying claims |
| 1 | Unsubstantiated claims; no methodology; contradicts other sources; errors present |

**Accuracy Evaluation Dimensions:**

**Methodology Transparency:**
✓ Sample size and composition clearly stated
✓ Data collection methods explained
✓ Analysis techniques specified
✓ Confidence intervals or margin of error provided
✓ Limitations and potential biases acknowledged

**Citation Quality:**
✓ Sources cited for factual claims
✓ Citations are traceable to original sources
✓ Recent, authoritative sources used
✓ No circular citations (A cites B, B cites A back)

**Internal Consistency:**
✓ Numbers add up correctly
✓ Charts match stated figures
✓ Conclusions follow from evidence
✓ No contradictions within document

**External Validation:**
✓ Key findings align with other reputable sources
✓ Numbers fall within reasonable range of similar studies
✓ Methodology is standard practice for field

**Presentation Quality:**
✓ Professional editing (few typos/errors)
✓ Clear, objective language
✓ Logical organization
✓ High-quality visualizations

**Red Flags:**
- No methodology section
- Extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence
- Contradicts multiple reliable sources without explanation
- No citations or only cites own organization
- Emotional or strongly biased language
- Math errors or inconsistent numbers
- Sweeping generalizations without data
- Cherry-picked data (showing favorable subset only)

**Verification Process:**

1. **Check primary claims:** Attempt to verify 2-3 key statistics with independent sources
2. **Methodology review:** Read methodology section; assess appropriateness
3. **Math verification:** Spot-check calculations if methodology provided
4. **Triangulation:** Compare findings with 2+ independent sources
5. **Expert sanity check:** Do conclusions seem reasonable to domain expert?

### Purpose: Reason Information Exists

**Definition:** Why does this information exist? Is the purpose to inform, persuade, sell, or entertain? Are there conflicts of interest?

**Evaluation Questions:**

✓ What is the purpose of the information? (Inform, teach, sell, persuade, entertain?)
✓ Do the authors/sponsors make their intentions clear?
✓ Is the information fact, opinion, or propaganda?
✓ Are there political, ideological, cultural, or commercial biases?
✓ Is the point of view objective and impartial?
✓ Are alternative viewpoints considered?
✓ Does the source acknowledge limitations?

**Scoring Criteria:**

| Score | Purpose Assessment |
|-------|---------------------|
| 5 | Purely informational; no commercial agenda; balanced view; limitations acknowledged |
| 4 | Primarily informational; minor promotional elements; mostly objective |
| 3 | Mixed informational/promotional; clear bias but useful data; requires context |
| 2 | Primarily promotional; significant bias; data selected to support agenda |
| 1 | Propaganda/marketing; extreme bias; unreliable due to agenda |

**Purpose Categories:**

**Inform (Best for Research):**
- Government statistical reports
- Academic research papers
- Independent research organizations
- Journalism from quality outlets

**Teach/Explain (Good for Research):**
- Educational content from universities
- Training materials from professional organizations
- Explanatory journalism

**Persuade (Use with Caution):**
- Think tank reports (may have ideological bent)
- Industry association advocacy documents
- Op-eds and opinion pieces
- Policy white papers

**Sell (High Bias Risk):**
- Vendor white papers
- Company marketing materials
- Sponsored research
- Advertorials

**Entertain (Not Suitable):**
- Blog posts for engagement
- Social media content
- Satirical content

**Bias Identification:**

**Commercial Bias:**
- Vendor-sponsored research favoring their solution
- Industry association downplaying regulation need
- Company reports minimizing risks, highlighting opportunities

**Ideological Bias:**
- Think tanks with clear political leanings
- Advocacy organizations with mission-driven research
- Policy proposals from partisan sources

**Geographic Bias:**
- National statistics emphasizing domestic advantages
- Regional development reports overestimating growth
- Local chambers of commerce overstating business climate

**Selection Bias:**
- Research highlighting successes, hiding failures
- Case studies of best-case scenarios
- Surveys of self-selected respondents

**Red Flags:**
- Funding source not disclosed
- Vendor logo on "independent" research
- Methodology designed to produce desired conclusion
- Alternative viewpoints not considered
- Extreme language or emotional appeals
- Disclaimers reveal hidden agendas
- No acknowledgment of limitations

**Disclosure Assessment:**

Evaluate transparency:
✓ Funding sources clearly stated
✓ Author affiliations disclosed
✓ Conflicts of interest acknowledged
✓ Limitations section present
✓ Alternative interpretations considered

**Applying Purpose Assessment:**

**When source has clear bias but useful data:**
1. Acknowledge bias in your research
2. Validate key statistics with independent sources
3. Use for context, not primary evidence
4. Quote with attribution (e.g., "According to [vendor name], which has commercial interest...")

**When source purpose undermines credibility:**
1. Seek alternative sources
2. If no alternative, state limitation explicitly
3. Triangulate heavily with independent verification

## Six Essential Questions Framework

Professional market research framework for evaluating secondary data quality. Used by commercial research firms to assess data appropriateness.

### Question 1: Who Collected the Data?

**Purpose:** Assess credibility and potential bias

**Evaluate:**
- Organization reputation and track record
- Researcher credentials and expertise
- Institutional affiliations
- Previous work quality
- Industry standing

**Red flags:**
- Unknown or recently formed organization
- No track record in this research area
- Researchers lack relevant expertise
- Organization has financial stake in findings

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Established research organization
- Recognized experts in field
- Independent from commercial interests
- Strong publication history

### Question 2: What Was the Purpose of Data Collection?

**Purpose:** Identify potential bias from original intent

**Evaluate:**
- Original research objectives
- Funding source and sponsor
- Intended audience
- Commercial vs. academic vs. policy intent

**Red flags:**
- Purpose misaligned with your research needs
- Commercial sponsor with vested interest
- Advocacy purpose rather than objective inquiry
- Purpose not clearly stated

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Academic research for knowledge advancement
- Government data for policy/planning
- Independent market research
- Purpose clearly stated and appropriate

### Question 3: When Were the Data Collected?

**Purpose:** Assess currency and relevance to current conditions

**Evaluate:**
- Data collection date (may differ from publication date)
- Time period covered
- Frequency of updates
- Market changes since collection

**Red flags:**
- Data collection predates major market shifts
- Long lag between collection and publication
- No updates available
- Seasonal data from wrong season

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Recent collection (within appropriate timeframe)
- Regular updates available
- Time period clearly documented
- Aligns with analysis timeframe

### Question 4: How Were the Data Collected?

**Purpose:** Evaluate methodology soundness and appropriateness

**Evaluate:**
- Sampling methodology (random, stratified, convenience)
- Sample size and composition
- Data collection instruments (survey, observation, experiment)
- Response rate (for surveys)
- Quality control measures
- Analysis techniques

**Red flags:**
- Methodology not disclosed
- Inappropriate sampling (convenience sample for generalizable claims)
- Small sample size for claimed precision
- Low response rate without non-response analysis
- Leading questions in surveys
- Unvalidated instruments

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Methodology fully transparent
- Appropriate sampling for research objectives
- Adequate sample size for claimed confidence level
- High response rate (>60% for surveys)
- Validated data collection instruments
- Clear analysis procedures

**Common Methodological Issues:**

**Sampling Problems:**
- Self-selection bias (online opt-in surveys)
- Survivorship bias (only analyzing successes)
- Convenience sampling presented as representative
- Underpowered sample for subgroup analysis

**Measurement Problems:**
- Leading or ambiguous survey questions
- Unvalidated scales
- Recall bias (asking about distant past)
- Social desirability bias (sensitive topics)

**Analysis Problems:**
- Correlation presented as causation
- Cherry-picked results
- Multiple testing without correction
- Inappropriate statistical techniques

### Question 5: What Specific Data Were Collected?

**Purpose:** Ensure data actually measures what you need

**Evaluate:**
- Variable definitions and operationalization
- Unit of analysis (individual, household, company, etc.)
- Geographic granularity
- Time granularity
- Industry/sector categorization
- Measurement scales and units

**Red flags:**
- Vague variable definitions
- Different definition than you need (e.g., "household" vs. "individual")
- Insufficient geographic detail
- Aggregated data when need segments
- Industry classifications don't match

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Clear, documented variable definitions
- Appropriate unit of analysis for your needs
- Sufficient granularity for required analysis
- Standard industry classifications (NAICS, SIC)
- Measurement units clearly specified

**Definition Mismatches:**

Common misalignments to watch for:
- **Market size:** Revenue vs. unit sales vs. shipments
- **Employment:** Full-time equivalents vs. headcount
- **Household:** Single-person vs. multi-person
- **Industry:** Different classification schemes
- **Region:** Different geographic boundaries

### Question 6: Are the Data Consistent with Other Sources?

**Purpose:** Triangulate and validate findings

**Evaluate:**
- Comparison with similar studies
- Alignment with related indicators
- Direction of trends vs. other sources
- Magnitude of estimates vs. other sources
- Expert opinion alignment

**Red flags:**
- Outlier compared to similar research (without explanation)
- Contradicts multiple credible sources
- Trend direction opposite other indicators
- Order of magnitude different from benchmarks
- No comparable sources exist (untestable)

**Best answer characteristics:**
- Findings align with 2+ independent sources
- Within reasonable range of similar studies
- Differences are explainable by methodology
- Expert consensus supports findings

**Triangulation Strategies:**

**Statistical triangulation:**
- Compare market size from different methods (top-down vs. bottom-up)
- Check if growth rates imply reasonable future sizes
- Verify consistency across time periods

**Source triangulation:**
- Government data + academic research + commercial research
- Supplier-side data + customer-side data
- Financial data + operational data

**Indicator triangulation:**
- If employment up, revenue should be up (unless productivity changed)
- If market growing, related markets should show signals
- Macro indicators should align with micro findings

**Resolving Inconsistencies:**

When sources disagree:
1. Check definitions (may be measuring different things)
2. Check time periods (may reflect different moments)
3. Check methodology (may have different biases)
4. Check sample (may reflect different populations)
5. Assess relative credibility (Tier 1 > Tier 2 > Tier 3)
6. If unresolvable, state range and confidence level

## Data Triangulation Framework

**Definition:** Using multiple independent sources to validate findings

**Origin:** Social science research methodology; adopted by consulting firms as quality standard

### Four Types of Triangulation

#### 1. Methodological Triangulation

**Concept:** Combine quantitative and qualitative methods

**Application:**
- Quantitative: Market sizing from statistical databases
- Qualitative: Customer interviews for needs assessment
- Integration: Numbers + context = insight

**Example:**
- Government statistics: Healthcare market $4.1T
- Academic surveys: 78% report access issues
- Synthesis: Large market with significant unmet needs

#### 2. Data Source Triangulation

**Concept:** Compare data from different groups, contexts, or time periods

**Application:**
- Different stakeholders: Customers, competitors, experts
- Different geographies: Regional comparison
- Different time periods: Trend validation

**Example:**
- Customer survey: "Price is top concern" (67%)
- Sales rep interviews: "Customers negotiate heavily on price"
- Win/loss analysis: Price mentioned in 73% of lost deals
- Synthesis: High confidence that price sensitivity is primary barrier

#### 3. Analyst Triangulation

**Concept:** Multiple researchers independently interpret same data

**Application:**
- Different team members review findings
- Independent validation of conclusions
- Peer review process

**Benefits:**
- Reduces individual bias
- Catches errors or misinterpretations
- Strengthens confidence in conclusions

#### 4. Theoretical Triangulation

**Concept:** Apply multiple theoretical frameworks to interpret findings

**Application:**
- Economic theory: Supply/demand analysis
- Behavioral theory: Customer psychology
- Strategic theory: Competitive positioning
- Integration: Multi-dimensional understanding

**Example:**
- Economic lens: Market at equilibrium price point
- Behavioral lens: Customers exhibit loss aversion
- Strategic lens: Low switching costs enable competition
- Synthesis: Price competition will intensify; differentiation required

### Triangulation Success Criteria

**Convergence (High Confidence):**
- 3+ independent sources confirm finding
- Different methodologies yield similar conclusions
- Consistency across time periods and geographies
- Expert consensus supports conclusion

**Action:** Use as high-confidence primary evidence

**Divergence (Low Confidence):**
- Sources contradict each other
- Wide range of estimates
- No clear consensus
- Unexplained discrepancies

**Action:** Investigate reasons for divergence, reassess research direction, or acknowledge uncertainty

### Triangulation Documentation

For critical findings, document:

**Finding:** [Key insight or statistic]

**Source 1:** [Citation] - [Supporting evidence]
**Source 2:** [Citation] - [Supporting evidence]
**Source 3:** [Citation] - [Supporting evidence]

**Assessment:**
☐ **Convergent:** High confidence, sources align
☐ **Partially convergent:** Moderate confidence, general agreement with minor differences
☐ **Divergent:** Low confidence, sources conflict

**Explanation of discrepancies:** [If any]

**Confidence level:** High / Medium / Low

## Common Data Quality Issues

### Issue 1: Outdated Data

**Problem:** Data predates significant market changes
**Detection:** Check publication and collection dates
**Solution:** Apply documented growth rates; validate with recent indicators; state limitation

### Issue 2: Mismatched Definitions

**Problem:** Source defines variable differently than needed
**Detection:** Read methodology section carefully
**Solution:** Adjust if methodology allows; find alternative source; acknowledge limitation

### Issue 3: Biased Sampling

**Problem:** Sample not representative of population
**Detection:** Review sampling methodology
**Solution:** Treat as directional only; seek probabilistic sample; weight if demographics known

### Issue 4: Undisclosed Methodology

**Problem:** No information on how data collected
**Detection:** Look for methodology section
**Solution:** Treat as low-credibility; validate heavily; seek better source

### Issue 5: Circular Citations

**Problem:** Sources cite each other, no original source
**Detection:** Trace citation chain back
**Solution:** Find primary source; if none exists, flag as unverified

### Issue 6: Aggregation Mismatch

**Problem:** Data too aggregated for needs (e.g., "North America" when need "US")
**Detection:** Check geographic/industry granularity
**Solution:** Find more granular source; estimate proportion if justified

### Issue 7: Survivorship Bias

**Problem:** Analysis only includes successful cases
**Detection:** Check if failures included
**Solution:** Adjust estimates downward; find balanced dataset

### Issue 8: Publication Bias

**Problem:** Only positive/significant results published
**Detection:** Check if null findings mentioned
**Solution:** Interpret effect sizes conservatively; seek meta-analyses

## Validation Checklist

Before citing any source as primary evidence, verify:

### Credibility
☐ Author/organization credentials verified
☐ CRAAP score ≥18 (good or excellent)
☐ Tier 1 or Tier 2 source category
☐ No major red flags identified

### Currency
☐ Published within appropriate timeframe for topic
☐ No major market shifts since data collection
☐ Updates available or planned

### Methodology
☐ Data collection methods documented
☐ Sample size adequate for claims
☐ Limitations acknowledged
☐ Methodology appropriate for research objectives

### Relevance
☐ Geographic coverage matches needs
☐ Industry/sector alignment confirmed
☐ Variable definitions match requirements
☐ Appropriate level of detail

### Validation
☐ Key findings verified with 2+ independent sources
☐ Findings align with expert opinion
☐ No unexplained discrepancies with benchmarks
☐ Confidence level documented

### Documentation
☐ Full citation prepared for bibliography
☐ CRAAP score recorded
☐ Triangulation sources identified
☐ Limitations noted for reporting
