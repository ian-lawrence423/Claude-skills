# Investment Evaluation Framework
## The Six Screening Questions

> This document defines the analytical standard for evaluating any investment opportunity. Each question is a gate, not a checkbox. An investment that cannot answer all six questions with specificity and evidence does not pass screening — regardless of how compelling the answer to any single question appears. The questions are sequenced deliberately: company quality precedes sector timing, which precedes investment attractiveness, which precedes exit realization, which precedes owner fit, which precedes adversarial diligence. A poor company in the right sector at the right price is still a poor company. A good company at the right price with the wrong owner is still a bad investment.

---

## Question 1: Why Is This a Good Company?

*The standard: a company that generates superior returns on capital over a full economic cycle, does something genuinely difficult to replicate, delivers measurable value to customers, and has unit economics that are individually profitable, structurally recurring, and outcome-aligned with the customer.*

Every Gate 1 assessment must address each of the following twelve criteria explicitly.
Company-level factors only. Sector-level factors belong in Gate 2.

### 1.1 Return on Invested Capital Across Cycles

**What to establish:** Whether ROIC is structurally high or cyclically elevated — and whether it has been sustained through adversity, not just achieved during expansion.

- Pull ROIC across a minimum of 10 years, explicitly spanning at least one economic contraction relevant to the sector. A track record built entirely during expansion years is insufficient.
- Decompose ROIC into its drivers: asset turnover and net margin (DuPont). Understand whether ROIC is high because the business is capital-light, because margins are structurally elevated, or both. Each has different durability characteristics.
- Map ROIC against the company's cost of capital over the same period. Sustained ROIC above WACC is value creation; ROIC below WACC — even at attractive absolute levels — is value destruction.
- Assess the stability of ROIC through the cycle, not just the average. A company with ROIC of 18% in expansion and 4% in contraction is a different asset than one with ROIC of 14% in both. The trough matters more than the peak.
- Cross-reference against the best public comparables in the sector. If ROIC is significantly above the peer median, identify exactly why — and assess whether that advantage is structural or transient.

**Red flags:** ROIC expansion coinciding entirely with a sector tailwind period; ROIC that improves in reported figures but deteriorates when lease obligations, working capital build, or capitalized costs are normalized; peer-relative ROIC advantage that cannot be traced to a specific structural differentiator.

---

### 1.2 Long-Term Historical Performance

**What to establish:** A complete, normalized operating history — not the curated version management presents. Revenue, EBITDA, margins, capex intensity, and key operating statistics (volume, price, mix) across full cycles.

- Reconstruct revenue growth disaggregated into volume, price, and mix for each year available. Revenue growth driven by price increases alone is a different risk profile than growth driven by volume expansion.
- Map EBITDA margin evolution over the same period. Is the margin trend structural (improving unit economics, operational leverage) or cyclical (favorable input costs, pricing tailwind that has since reversed)?
- Normalize reported EBITDA for: owner compensation above or below market, one-time items management characterizes as non-recurring but recur in practice, capitalized costs that should be expensed, and maintenance capex embedded in growth capex reporting.
- Assess capex intensity relative to revenue growth. A business that requires $1 of capex to generate $1 of incremental revenue is fundamentally different from one requiring $0.10. Calculate the capex-to-revenue ratio over the full period and identify whether it is rising, stable, or declining.
- Review key operating statistics (KPIs) over the same period — occupancy rates, seats, units shipped, active customers, transaction volume, whatever the relevant operating driver is. Operational KPI trends should be consistent with the financial narrative. Where they diverge, understand why.

**Red flags:** Margin expansion in the most recent 2–3 years that is not supported by a structural change in the business; revenue growth that looks consistent but masks a deteriorating mix (more volume at lower price, or lower-margin products growing faster); working capital build that signals revenue recognition ahead of cash collection.

### 1.3 Competitive Advantage and Differentiation

**What to establish:** What the company does that others cannot easily replicate, whether that advantage is structural or eroding, and the realistic displacement path a well-resourced competitor would need to execute.

- Name the specific source of advantage: cost (structural cost position, not just operational efficiency), service (superiority that customers validate, not management asserts), technology (proprietary capability with defensible IP or data moats), distribution (channel access or network density that takes years to build), brand (demonstrated pricing premium or preference in primary research), or network effects (explicit description of the network mechanism and evidence that it compounds).
- Assess sustainability horizon. A competitive advantage that erodes within 3–5 years is not a thesis; it is a window. For each named advantage, state explicitly: what would have to happen for a well-funded competitor to match it, and how long would that take? If the answer is "18 months and $50M," the advantage is not a moat.
- Map the displacement scenario: identify the top 2–3 realistic threats (existing competitors with adjacent capability, well-funded new entrants, large platform players who could absorb this capability), and for each, assess: what they would have to build or acquire, what switching cost the customer would have to absorb, and whether there are historical examples of this displacement occurring elsewhere in the sector.
- Validate the advantage through primary research — customers, former employees, and channel partners — not management characterization. Management will always assert differentiation; customers will tell you whether they actually pay for it.

**Red flags:** Competitive advantage described only in terms of relationships or service culture — both of which are highly portable; technology advantage that has not been renewed with investment and faces newer architectures from competitors; market share that is stable but only because the company has repeatedly lowered price to defend it.

### 1.4 Value Delivered to Customers

**What to establish:** The specific problem this company solves, how acutely the customer experiences it, and whether the value delivered is quantifiable and validated by primary research — not management's characterization of it.

- Define the customer's job-to-be-done precisely. What situation prompts the customer to seek this product or service? What outcome are they trying to achieve? What are the functional, emotional, and economic dimensions of that outcome?
- Quantify the value delivered wherever possible. Cost savings delivered relative to the alternative; revenue uplift attributable to using this product; time saved and its economic equivalent; risk reduced and its insurance-equivalent value. Prefer quantified value over qualitative characterizations.
- Distinguish between value that is felt acutely (the customer notices immediately when it is absent) and value that is ambient (the customer perceives it over time). Mission-critical, acutely felt value drives higher willingness to pay and lower churn. Ambient value does not.
- Conduct primary research with a representative sample of customers — not a selection curated by management. Specifically: ask customers what they would do if this product were no longer available, what they would pay for that alternative, and whether they have recently evaluated alternatives. Revealed preference is more reliable than stated satisfaction.
- Assess customer NPS or equivalent satisfaction metrics — but treat them as a leading indicator, not a conclusion. A high NPS in a market with no alternatives means less than a high NPS in a competitive market. Contextualize.

**Red flags:** Value proposition that customers describe in vague terms ("it makes things easier") rather than specific outcome terms; high customer satisfaction scores coinciding with high churn, which signals satisfaction is being measured at the wrong moment in the relationship; primary research that was facilitated by management (customer list was provided by the company, interviews were pre-briefed, or only reference accounts were made available).

### 1.5 Unit Economics

*All three tests must pass. Passing two of three is not a partial credit — it is a different risk profile that requires specific explanation before proceeding.*

**Test 1 — Profitable on a customer basis**

- Calculate gross margin per customer, segmented by customer cohort, size tier, and acquisition channel. Blended gross margin can obscure a situation where large anchor customers are highly profitable but SMB or mid-market customers are margin-negative.
- Assess whether gross margin per customer improves or degrades with scale and tenure. The thesis for most subscription and services businesses is that gross margin improves as the customer matures (onboarding costs amortize, expansion occurs within existing infrastructure). Validate this with cohort-level data.
- If the company earns negative gross margin on any meaningful customer segment, identify the stated rationale (land-and-expand, market-share prioritization, onboarding investment) and assess whether the historical evidence supports it — specifically, whether early-cohort negative-margin customers have grown into positive-margin accounts at the rate and timeline management projected.

**Test 2 — Recurring or re-occurring revenue**

- Classify revenue precisely. Contractually recurring (SaaS subscription, maintenance contract, retainer): the customer must actively cancel to stop paying. Behaviorally re-occurring (high repurchase rate, mission-critical workflow dependency, high switching cost): the customer does not renew contractually but reliably repurchases. Transactional (volume-dependent, project-based, spot): revenue requires an active customer decision each period.
- These are different risk profiles and should be named explicitly. Recurring and re-occurring businesses typically command higher multiples than transactional ones — but an investor who misclassifies behavioral re-occurrence as contractual recurrence is carrying hidden churn risk.
- Calculate gross revenue retention (GRR) and net revenue retention (NRR) separately, by cohort where possible. GRR is the downside floor; NRR is the expansion potential ceiling. A business with GRR of 85% and NRR of 115% has a different risk profile than one with GRR of 95% and NRR of 100% — even if the headline retention metric looks similar.

**Test 3 — Scalable with the customer (outcome-oriented pricing)**

- Assess whether the pricing model aligns with the value the customer receives. Does revenue grow as the customer grows or as the customer derives more value? Models where price scales with seats, usage, percentage of spend managed, or transaction volume are structurally better positioned to grow revenue without requiring new customer acquisition.
- Identify the ceiling on the pricing model: is there a natural cap on revenue per customer (a seat limit, a volume tier that plateaus, a budget constraint)? Companies where revenue per customer is contractually capped or where the pricing model does not scale with customer growth must acquire new customers to grow — which has a different cost structure and growth quality.
- Assess pricing relative to value delivered. If the company is charging significantly below the economic value it delivers to customers, there is pricing power available — but only if the market structure and competitive dynamics allow capture of it. Quantify the gap between value delivered and price charged, and assess what is required to close it.

**Reconciliation test:** A company with strong ROIC but weak unit economics is a historical story, not a forward thesis — the ROIC was generated under conditions that the unit economics suggest cannot be maintained. A company with strong unit economics but poor ROIC history has an execution problem to explain — either the capital allocation has been poor, or the unit economics are more fragile than they appear at the cohort level. Both require specific, substantiated answers before proceeding.

### 1.6 Management Quality and Organizational Depth

**What to establish:** Whether the leadership team has the capability to run this business through its next phase of development — not whether they built something impressive in a favorable environment.

- Assess whether the CEO and senior leadership team have managed through a full business cycle, including a period of operational stress. A team that has only operated during a growth cycle has not been tested on the decisions that matter most: capital rationing, organizational right-sizing, pricing defense under competitive pressure, and creditor/investor communication under stress.
- Evaluate the fit between the team's archetype and the business's current stage. A founder-CEO who excels at building from zero to scale may be the wrong operator for a business that needs margin optimization and operational discipline. A financial operator may be wrong for a business that needs product-led growth. Name the archetype and assess whether it fits.
- Assess depth of the second tier. Can the business be run if the top one or two people departed? This is not an abstract question — it is a diligence item. Map the roles, assess the bench, and identify key-person concentration explicitly.
- Review capital allocation decisions over the full operating history: M&A transactions, capex programs, significant hiring cycles, pricing changes. For each major allocation decision, assess whether it proved directionally correct in hindsight and what it reveals about the management team's judgment.
- Validate through structured reference checks with former employees, customers, and counterparties — not management-provided references. Ask specifically about decisions made under pressure, how the team behaves when results disappoint expectations, and how they treat people who disagree with them.

**Red flags:** A management team whose track record is largely attributable to a sector tailwind rather than operational choices; a CEO whose personal equity is a small fraction of their total compensation (weak alignment); high senior leadership turnover in the 12–18 months prior to the deal process.

### 1.7 Market Structure and Competitive Position

**What to establish:** Whether the company occupies a structurally attractive position in its market, or whether it is a competent operator in a structurally difficult one.

- Assess whether this is a market where scale advantages compound or one where fragmentation persists. In markets where scale creates durable advantages (data network effects, purchasing leverage, brand density, cost-per-unit decline), the leader's position becomes more defensible over time. In fragmented markets, scale provides limited protection and position must be continuously defended.
- Determine whether the company is a price-setter or a price-taker. Price-setters have demonstrated the ability to raise prices without material volume loss. Price-takers operate in markets where price is determined by competitive dynamics or commodity inputs. This distinction matters enormously for margin durability.
- Quantify market share and assess the trend. A company with 40% share in a growing market is a different investment from one with 40% share in a contracting market — even at the same entry multiple. Map share trends over 5+ years.
- Assess customer concentration explicitly. If the top 3 customers represent more than 30% of revenue, the market structure creates structural leverage risk regardless of the company's overall quality. Map revenue by customer decile and assess the trend toward or away from concentration.
- Apply Porter's Five Forces to assess industry attractiveness as a structural matter — not just the company's current position. A high-quality company in a structurally unattractive industry (high buyer power, low switching costs, commoditizing technology, low barriers to entry) faces a structural headwind that will compound over time.

### 1.8 Financial Resilience and Downside Durability

**What to establish:** How the business behaves under stress — and whether the downside scenario is survivable and recoverable without destroying the equity.

- Reconstruct performance during the most relevant prior stress period for this sector: the 2008–2009 financial crisis, the 2020 COVID shock, a sector-specific downturn, or an interest rate cycle, depending on which is most analogous. How did revenue, EBITDA, margins, and cash flow behave? Did the business recover, and how quickly?
- Identify the cash flow break-even revenue level. What is the minimum revenue at which the business generates zero free cash flow, before debt service? This defines the margin of safety.
- Assess the variable versus fixed cost structure. A business with 60% fixed costs has high operating leverage — it compounds returns in expansion but amplifies losses in contraction. A business with primarily variable costs is more resilient but has less operational leverage. Name which applies and assess the implications for the downside scenario.
- Determine whether the customer base is defensive or cyclical. In a macroeconomic downturn: do customers cut this spend first or last? Is the product essential to the customer's operations, or discretionary? For B2B customers, is the product tied to the customer's headcount, revenue, or transaction volume — and what does that mean in a volume-down scenario?
- Stress-test the capital structure. At what level of EBITDA decline does the business breach covenants or face a liquidity event? Build a specific downside scenario — not a "sensitivity" — and assess the equity outcome explicitly.

---

### 1.9 Path to Continued Value Creation

**What to establish:** That the forward thesis is as rigorous as the historical one — with specific, sized, and conditioned levers, not a generic growth narrative.

- Map each organic lever explicitly: pricing power relative to current realization (what is the gap between value delivered and price charged?); cross-sell and upsell attach rate relative to the theoretical ceiling (what percentage of customers have adopted the adjacent product?); white space geographies or verticals with named TAM and a specific go-to-market plan to penetrate them.
- For each lever: size it (what is the realistic revenue or EBITDA impact?), time it (when does it materialize, and what is the ramp?), and condition it (what has to be true operationally, competitively, or in terms of market development for this lever to work?). Levers without stated conditions are assumptions masquerading as a plan.
- Assess M&A tuck-in optionality separately from organic growth. If M&A is a lever, map the acquisition pipeline: what types of targets, at what typical entry multiple, with what synergy thesis, and what track record of integration does the management team have? M&A optionality that depends on targets being available at attractive prices is not a lever — it is a hope.
- Distinguish between revenue growth that expands margins (operating leverage, mix shift toward higher-margin products, pricing realization) and revenue growth that is margin-neutral or dilutive. Only margin-accretive revenue growth creates EBITDA growth at a faster rate than revenue growth — which is the mechanism for multiple expansion and ROIC improvement simultaneously.

---

### 1.10 Customer Concentration and Cohort Behavior

**What to establish:** That the revenue base is durable at the customer level — not just at the aggregate — and that older cohorts are not decaying in ways the blended metrics obscure.

- Map revenue concentration by customer decile. Assess whether concentration is increasing or decreasing over time and understand the mechanism: is the top decile growing faster because of genuine expansion, or is the rest of the base churning?
- Examine cohort retention longitudinally. Pull GRR and NRR by annual cohort for at least 5 years where data exists. The critical test: do cohorts launched 3+ years ago retain at the same rate as newer cohorts? Cohort decay — where older cohorts show accelerating churn relative to newer ones — is a leading indicator of product-market fit erosion or competitive displacement of the installed base.
- Assess the expansion mechanic within cohorts. NRR above 100% means the cohort expands revenue over time even as some customers churn. Understand exactly what drives the expansion: new products adopted, volume growth, price increases, or seat expansion. Each has a different durability profile and ceiling.
- For high-concentration businesses (top customer >15% of revenue), stress-test the customer relationship directly. How long is the contract? Is it auto-renewing or must it be affirmatively renewed? Is the relationship with an individual decision-maker (key-person risk) or embedded in the customer's systems and workflows (structural stickiness)? When was the last competitive RFP, and what was the outcome?

## Question 2: Why Is This a Good Sector to Invest In Today?

*The standard: a sector where the structural characteristics are genuinely attractive — not merely growing — and where a specific dynamic creates a genuine entry window right now. The sector thesis must stand independently of the specific company and independently of the stock price or valuation. The timing thesis must be as precise as the company thesis, and the consequences of the timing being wrong must be explicitly modeled.*

### Canonical Sector Criteria — Reference Checklist

Every Gate 2 assessment must address each of the following twelve criteria explicitly.
A sector thesis that omits a criterion is incomplete. Criteria that are unfavorable must
be disclosed, not suppressed — the purpose of this checklist is to surface structural
weaknesses before they surface post-investment.

**What belongs here vs. other gates:**

> Gate 2 answers: is this sector worth being in? The answer must be true for any
> well-positioned player in the sector, not just this specific company.
>
> **What does NOT belong in Gate 2:**
> - Stock price drawdown, valuation discount, or sentiment mismatch → Gate 3 (investment attractiveness)
> - Company-specific competitive position, moat, or management → Gate 1 (company quality)
> - Adverse selection thesis or why the opportunity exists at this price → Gate 6
>
> **The test:** Could you make the same Gate 2 argument for a private company in the
> same sector with no public market price? If yes, it is a sector argument. If it
> requires referencing a stock price or market valuation, it belongs in Gate 3.

### 2.1 The Entry Window: What Is True Today That Was Not True Before?

**What to establish:** The specific macro or industry dynamic that creates an entry opportunity at this moment — and why that window exists now rather than one year ago or one year from now.

- Name the dynamic precisely. Do not accept generic formulations ("the sector is growing" or "digitization is accelerating"). The specific trigger should be one of: a regulatory inflection (new rule or enforcement priority that changes competitive dynamics or market size), a technology inflection (a new capability that enables a business model that was previously impossible or uneconomic), a demand inflection (a structural shift in buyer behavior or willingness to spend, with evidence from recent data), a competitive inflection (a dominant incumbent weakening, a failed consolidation attempt, or a new category emerging from a previously monolithic market), or a capital structure inflection (a dislocation in credit markets, a period of sector-wide distress, or a valuation reset that creates relative value).
- Distinguish between secular tailwinds (which are always present and therefore do not create a specific entry window) and cyclical or event-driven dynamics (which are time-limited and therefore constitute a genuine window). The entry window thesis must be based on the latter.
- Quantify the window. How long is it open — 12 months, 36 months, indefinitely? What event would close it (a competitor capturing the opportunity, a regulatory reversal, a credit cycle turning)? The tighter the window, the more precise the execution must be and the more important the timing of capital deployment.
- Assess whether the window is widely recognized or not yet consensus. A widely recognized entry window is already partially priced. The most attractive windows are those where the underlying dynamic is real but not yet reflected in asset valuations — which requires a specific view on why the market has not yet priced it.

### 2.2 Macro and Industry Dynamics Driving the Window

**What to establish:** The structural forces — macro, regulatory, technological, and demographic — that support the sector over the investment hold period, and whether those forces are strengthening, stable, or beginning to reverse.

- **Macroeconomic alignment:** Does the current macro environment (interest rate trajectory, GDP growth expectations, credit availability, consumer confidence) favor this sector? Assess explicitly whether the sector is early-cycle, mid-cycle, or late-cycle sensitive — and map where we are in that cycle. A sector that performs best in early-cycle conditions is a different entry decision in early 2024 than in late 2021.
- **Regulatory environment:** Is regulation moving in a direction that expands the addressable market, reduces competitive threats from new entrants, or creates compliance-driven demand for what this sector provides? Alternatively, is there regulatory risk — pending legislation, enforcement posture changes, or litigation — that could compress margins or market size? Identify the 2–3 specific regulatory developments that matter most and assess their probability and timeline.
- **Technology cycle:** Is this sector in the early, middle, or late stage of a technology adoption curve? Early-stage technology sectors offer growth potential but carry adoption risk. Late-stage sectors offer more predictable growth but face disruption risk from next-generation technology. Assess which technologies could disrupt the current market structure within the investment hold period and what the probability and timeline are.
- **Demographic and behavioral shifts:** Are there demographic or behavioral changes that structurally expand demand for what this sector provides over the hold period? Be specific: name the demographic trend, quantify its impact on sector demand, and assess whether it is secular (durable) or cyclical (temporary).
- **Supply dynamics:** Is supply in this sector constrained, expanding, or rationalizing? In capital-intensive sectors, supply constraints drive pricing power and margin expansion. Supply rationalization (consolidation, exit of marginal players) typically improves sector economics for remaining players. Excess supply — from over-investment in prior cycles — is a margin headwind. Identify the current supply dynamic and its trajectory.

---

### 2.3 Competitive Landscape Inflection

**What to establish:** Whether the current competitive dynamics in the sector favor new investment — specifically, whether the incumbent competitive positions are being disrupted, consolidated, or reinforced in ways that create opportunity.

- Map the current competitive structure: concentrated (3–5 dominant players) or fragmented (many players, no dominant incumbent)? Is consolidation underway, and if so, is the opportunity to be an acquirer or to be acquired? Is there a dominant incumbent whose position is weakening — and if so, what is the mechanism and timeline?
- Identify whether this is a sector in the process of being redefined by a new competitive category. The most attractive entry windows often occur when a new business model is disrupting an established one — the established players are too slow or too invested in their existing model to respond, and the new entrant has not yet been fully valued by the market.
- Assess the current M&A environment in the sector. What have recent transactions priced at, and why? Is there a buyer/seller price expectation gap that is creating a holding pattern — and if so, is this expected to resolve in a way that creates entry opportunities at attractive valuations?
- Evaluate talent dynamics. In sectors undergoing disruption, talent flow is a leading indicator. Are the best operators leaving the incumbents and moving to challengers? Is there a war for talent that is compressing margins at scale, or is the sector generating more talent than it can absorb?

---

### 2.4 Valuation Entry Point

**What to establish:** Whether current sector valuations offer an attractive entry relative to the underlying fundamental quality — and whether the entry point is a function of intrinsic value or a transient market dynamic.

- Compare current sector trading multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E where applicable) to the 5-year, 10-year, and cycle-normalized historical ranges. Is the sector trading at a discount to history, and if so, is the discount explained by a temporary factor (rate environment, sentiment cycle, specific sector controversy) or a fundamental deterioration?
- Assess the relationship between current valuations and the sector's fundamental growth and return profile. A sector trading at 8x EBITDA with 15% EBITDA growth and 20% ROIC is objectively cheaper than a sector trading at 12x EBITDA with 8% EBITDA growth and 12% ROIC, even though the absolute multiple is lower.
- Identify whether there is a sentiment or narrative mismatch — where the market is applying a discount based on a perceived risk that is either overstated or temporary. These mismatches are the source of entry opportunity, but they require a specific thesis for why the market's view is wrong, not just a belief that the multiple will mean-revert.
- Assess the cost of capital environment relative to sector return profiles. In a higher-rate environment, sectors where returns are front-loaded and cash-flow-positive early are structurally more attractive than sectors where returns are back-weighted and require capital during the hold period.

---

### 2.5 What Happens If the Timing Is Wrong?

**What to establish:** The return outcome in the scenario where the entry window dynamic does not materialize as expected — and whether the investment is structurally protected or dependent on the timing thesis.

- Build a specific scenario where the key timing thesis does not materialize: the regulatory change is delayed 2 years, the technology adoption curve is slower than expected, the macro environment deteriorates before the sector benefits from it. In this scenario, what is the return outcome?
- Assess whether the investment is protected by company quality in the timing-wrong scenario. A high-quality company in a good sector, purchased at a reasonable price, should generate an acceptable return even if the specific timing thesis takes longer to materialize. An investment that requires the timing thesis to be precisely right is not a high-quality investment thesis — it is a trade.
- Identify the early warning indicators that the timing thesis is not developing as expected, and define the decision rule for each: at what point does a slower-than-expected development warrant a revised thesis versus an exit?
- Quantify the downside return explicitly. If the timing thesis is wrong and the investment horizon extends by 2 years, what is the IRR impact? What if the entry window closes permanently because a competitor captures the opportunity? Present the return distribution with the timing scenario modeled explicitly, not suppressed.

## Question 3: Why Is This a Good Investment?

*The standard: an IRR and MOIC that are attractive relative to the risk taken — not just the headline number. Value creation disaggregated by lever, with timing mapped. The return is earned, not assumed.*

### Canonical Investment Criteria — Reference Checklist

Gate 3 is the synthesis gate. It draws on Gate 1 (company quality) and Gate 2 (sector attractiveness) and combines them with valuation entry point, return structure, and exit realization. Nine factors must be addressed. Factors 1–3 should largely be answered by Gates 1 and 2 — if they cannot be, the investment fails the earlier gates and should not reach Gate 3. Factors 4–9 are Gate 3-specific.

**What belongs here vs. other gates:**

> Gate 3 answers: given what we know about the company (Gate 1) and the sector (Gate 2),
> does the investment — at this price, at this time, with this structure — generate
> attractive risk-adjusted returns?
>
> Valuation entry point, stock price drawdown, sentiment mismatch, and adverse selection
> thesis belong HERE — not in Gate 2. Gate 3 is where the price paid becomes the
> analytical subject.

### 3.2 Need-to-Believe Registry — Required Format

**Every IC memo must include a Need-to-Believe (NTB) registry in this exact structure,
positioned in the Executive Summary section immediately after the investment verdict
paragraph and before the gate table.**

The NTB registry makes the base case assumptions explicit, assesses their current
evidence state, links them to specific diligence actions, and quantifies the return
impact of each assumption failing or beating. It is the bridge between the investment
thesis (what we believe) and the returns disaggregation table (what each belief is worth).

**Relationship to other required sections:**
- NTB registry → upstream of returns disaggregation (section 3.3)
- Each NTB maps to one or more rows in the returns disaggregation table
- Each NTB maps verbatim to one or more items in the information gaps table
- NTB evidence states feed directly into position sizing logic

#### Required Columns

| Column | Definition | Construction rule |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **Need-to-Believe** | The governing base case assumption — one declarative sentence stating what must be true | Not a question, not a risk. A positive statement of what the investment requires. Number each NTB (NTB 1, NTB 2, etc.). |
| **Key Findings & Workstreams** | Bullet-point findings with evidence tags. Confirmed facts first, then conditional assessments, then explicitly flagged gaps | **GAP items must be prefixed `GAP:` in red bold text — not softened or buried in prose.** Every bullet carries an evidence tag [F], [E], or [H] and a named source or Info Gap reference. Minimum 6 bullets per NTB; gap bullets must include the specific Info Gap priority number. |
| **Evidence State** | Single tag: CONFIRMED (green) / CONDITIONAL (orange) / GAP (red) | CONFIRMED = primary data in hand confirming the assumption. CONDITIONAL = directional evidence but material data gap remains. GAP = assumption not yet testable from available data. |
| **CY[exit year]E EBITDA & MOIC Impact** | Absolute dollar EBITDA variance if NTB fails or beats, plus MOIC delta | Pulled directly from the returns disaggregation table. Must include a footnote disclosing: hold period, entry equity value, exit multiple, MOIC derivation formula. Note compound path impacts where single-driver table understates severity. |
| **Boundability** | B1 / B2 / B3 tag (color-coded) followed by a 3–5 sentence narrative | The tag alone is insufficient. The narrative must state: (1) what specific data would sharpen the assessment; (2) where the B2/B3 boundary lies within the NTB (some NTBs have split boundability — e.g., B2 for probability, B3 for timing); (3) for B3 components, name precisely what makes it unboundable. Do not suppress B3 items. |

**Note: Diligence Owner / Timeline is intentionally excluded from this table.** That information lives in the information gaps table, which is the authoritative source for diligence prioritization. The NTB table links to the information gaps table by reference number only (e.g., [Info Gap 1 — CRITICAL]). Duplicating diligence ownership in the NTB table creates maintenance risk when priorities change.

#### Required Footnote

Every NTB table must carry a footnote immediately below stating:

> *Returns impact assumptions: [X]-year hold from [date] entry. Entry equity value $[X]B at $[X]. Exit multiple [X]x [CY year]E EBITDA held constant across all NTB rows. MOIC delta = CY[year]E EBITDA variance ($M) × [X]x ÷ $[X]B. Single-driver movements only — compound failure paths produce non-linear outcomes not captured here. [CY year]E base case EBITDA: $[X]B. Pattern estimates [E].*

#### NTB Count and Coverage

- **Minimum 4 NTBs** for any investment thesis
- Every NTB must govern a material return driver (>5% of total MOIC)
- The sum of MOIC impacts across all NTBs must approximate the bull-to-bear MOIC spread
- Every GAP item must also appear in the information gaps table as a named priority
- B3 NTBs must be shown at full severity — they cannot be excluded because they are hard to quantify

#### Evidence State Classification Rules

**CONFIRMED:** Primary data (company filings, independent primary research, or confirmed expert
network validation) supports the assumption without material remaining uncertainty.

**CONDITIONAL:** At least one of the following applies:
- Data is directionally supportive but derives from sell-side research with potential confirmation bias
- A specific gap item remains unresolved that would materially change the assessment
- The assumption is sourced to [E] or [H] for a component representing >20% of the driver's MOIC impact

**GAP:** At least one of the following applies:
- The primary data required to test the assumption has not been obtained
- The assumption is hypothesis-level [H] without a Tier 1–2 source
- The data exists but is structurally inaccessible without active IR or expert network engagement

#### What Makes a Good NTB Statement

A good NTB statement is:
- **Falsifiable** — a specific observable would prove it wrong
- **Quantitatively linked** — there is a dollar amount of return at stake
- **Specific to the company** — not a generic sector claim that any company could make
- **Base case framing** — states what must be true, not what might go wrong

❌ Too generic: *"The market will grow."*
❌ Risk framing: *"TikTok competition does not escalate."*
✅ Correct: *"Indonesia GMV — 37% of SEA platform GMV — reaccelerates to 15–20% YoY in 2026 after 2025 structural noise, sustaining the base case volume assumption."*

#### Gate Scoring Table Format — Required for Gate 1, Gate 2, Gate 3

Every gate must be scored against its canonical criteria in a structured table. A narrative
assertion that "the company passes Gate 1" without a criterion-by-criterion scorecard does
not satisfy this requirement.

**Required table structure:**

| # | Criterion | Verdict | Evidence Assessment |
|---|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| 1.1 | [Criterion name] | PASS / CONDITIONAL / WATCH | 1–2 sentences with evidence tags and source |
| ... | | | |

**Verdict definitions:**
- **PASS** — criterion clearly satisfied with data in hand; a hostile IC member could not
  reasonably dispute this verdict given the evidence cited
- **CONDITIONAL** — directionally positive but a specific gap exists that would materially
  change the assessment if resolved adversely
- **WATCH** — criterion raises a material concern that is not offset by other criteria;
  must be addressed in the risk section and information gaps table

**Gate summary cell in the gate table:**
The gate table on the cover/exec summary must cross-reference the scorecard section —
it does not attempt to summarise all criteria inline. Format:
> "[X] of 12 criteria PASS; [Y] CONDITIONAL; [Z] WATCH. Full scorecard in Section [N]."

**Gate classification rules (enforced):**
- Gate 2 contains only sector-level analysis. Valuation, drawdown, sentiment mismatch,
  and adverse selection belong in Gate 3. Company-specific moat belongs in Gate 1.
  Test: "Could I make this same argument for a private company with no market price?"
  If no → move to the correct gate.

#### Investment Thesis Architecture — Five-Point NTB Structure (Required)

The investment thesis section must be structured around the Need-to-Believe points —
not written as independent segment or product analyses. Every NTB becomes a numbered
thesis point carrying all supporting analysis for that assumption.

**Required format for each thesis point:**

```
[Number]. [NTB statement — one sentence]  [NTB # — Evidence State / Boundability tag]

Paragraph 1: Confirmed evidence — lead with facts [F], then conditional assessments [E]
Paragraph 2: The specific uncertainty — name the gap, quantify its MOIC impact
DATA GAP callout (if applicable): exact unresolved primary data item, in orange left-border
Paragraph 3: Why it matters to the consolidated thesis — link to returns impact
[Supporting table or data note if material evidence warrants it]
```

**What this structure replaces:**
- Independent h2 sections for each business segment without a governing NTB
- Flywheel or moat narratives disconnected from return quantification
- Risk observations embedded in the thesis without a named MOIC impact

**Minimum:** One thesis point per NTB in the registry. If an NTB governs multiple
business segments, those segments are discussed under that NTB point — not in separate
sections of their own.

**Auditability standard:** An IC member who disagrees with the investment recommendation
should be able to identify precisely which thesis point they dispute, and that thesis point
should contain enough specific evidence (tagged, sourced, gap-flagged) for them to engage
with the data rather than just the conclusion.

### 3.3 Returns Disaggregation Table — Required Format

**Every IC memo must include a returns disaggregation table in this exact structure.
A bullet-point narrative of return drivers does not satisfy this requirement.**

The table operationalizes the profit tree decomposition from the analytical OS
(Unit Price × Unit Volume → Revenue; Fixed + Variable COGS; Fixed + Variable SG&A)
and applies the financial statement sense-check layer: for each projected trend,
confirm it is consistent with past performance, competitive position, market trends,
and investment in the business (R&D, Capex). Projections that fail the sense-check
are downside variances until confirmed by diligence.

#### Required Columns

| Column | Definition | Construction rule |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **Driver** | The specific value creation lever — one row per material driver | Use the profit tree decomposition: revenue (volume/price/mix), gross margin, OpEx leverage, working capital, exit multiple. Never aggregate multiple drivers into one row. |
| **Base Case Assumption** | The specific figure, rate, or metric the model uses | Must be a number or range, not a directional label. "Revenue grows" fails. "$127B GMV at 2.5% take-rate → $3.2B Shopee EBITDA" passes. |
| **Upside Scenario + Mechanism** | Named operational condition that produces the upside, plus the resulting figure | Must name the mechanism, not just the outcome. "Better execution" fails. "Ad take-rate reaches 3.5% because MELI analogy transfers at Shopee's AOV" passes. |
| **Downside Scenario + Mechanism** | Named operational condition that produces the downside, plus the resulting figure | Same standard. Mechanism must be specific and traceable to a named failure mode or observable. |
| **EBITDA Impact (Up / Down)** | Absolute dollar change in EBITDA if upside or downside materializes | State in $M. Derived as: (variance figure − base case figure) × relevant margin or multiple. This is the operating magnitude layer. |
| **MOIC Impact (Up / Down)** | Change in 3-year MOIC if that EBITDA variance flows through to exit | Derived as: EBITDA impact × exit multiple / entry equity value. State as +/− x.xx MOIC. This is the return layer. Do not use IRR here — MOIC is directly readable without a calculator. |
| **Resolving Diligence Item** | The specific observable, data request, or primary research that confirms or refutes the base case assumption | Must map to a named kill criterion or information gap in the IC memo. "Further diligence" fails. "Q1 2026 Indonesia GMV commentary — Shopee-specific rate" passes. |

#### Calibration Rules

- MOIC deltas must sum to the spread between the bull and bear case MOIC.
  If bull case is 3.0x and bear case is 1.3x, the rows must collectively account
  for the 1.7x spread across upside and downside variances.
- Each variance must name a mechanism, not a direction. No row may use
  "better/worse than expected" — the specific operational condition must be named.
- Each resolving diligence item must appear verbatim in the information gaps table.
  If it does not, either add it to the information gaps table or acknowledge it
  as an unresolved risk.
- Working capital row is mandatory for businesses where revenue growth drives
  meaningful working capital changes. FCF conversion at normalized growth rates
  must be stated, not assumed constant.
- Exit multiple row is mandatory. State the mechanism for re-rating (not just
  the target multiple) and the conditions under which it does not occur.

#### Derivation Disclosed in Document

The MOIC impact derivation must appear as a footnote to the table:

> MOIC impact derivation: EBITDA variance ($M) × exit multiple (x) ÷ entry
> equity value ($B) = MOIC delta. Entry equity value = market cap at time of
> analysis. Exit multiple held constant at base case for all rows except the
> exit multiple row itself, where EBITDA is held at base case.

This makes the table self-contained and auditable. A reader can verify any cell
without reconstructing the model.

#### Minimum Required Drivers

The following rows are required in every IC memo. Add company-specific rows
for any driver that represents more than 5% of total return:

1. Core revenue growth (volume × take-rate or price, by primary geography)
2. Gross margin / unit economics improvement (cost per order, COGS structure)
3. OpEx leverage (S&M, G&A as % of revenue at exit vs. entry)
4. Working capital dynamics (FCF conversion at normalized vs. current growth)
5. Secondary market / geography contribution (if >10% of total value)
6. Exit multiple (mechanism + conditions for re-rating)
7. Net cash / balance sheet contribution (net cash per share at exit)

#### What This Table Is Not

- It is not a sensitivity analysis. Sensitivity analysis holds all variables
  constant except one. This table holds all variables at base case except one
  per row, and adds a mechanism and diligence item for each. The distinction
  matters: sensitivity analysis tells you what the model is sensitive to;
  this table tells you why and how to resolve it.
- It is not a scenario analysis. The upside and downside variances are
  single-driver movements, not full scenario constructions. Full scenarios
  (bull/base/bear/tail) remain as a separate exhibit — this table decomposes
  the spread between them into attributable drivers.

### 3.4 Return Driver Taxonomy: Full Profit Tree

**What to establish:** The return model built from the bottom up — each profit driver sized, timed, and linked to available evidence. Build in two passes: (1) decompose IRR into four structural components — revenue growth, gross margin expansion, OpEx leverage, and net cash / leverage paydown — and size each; (2) break each component into granular drivers using the taxonomy below, assign evidence tier, and confirm the diligence workplan covers every material driver. A model assumption with no diligence workstream assigned is an unmanaged risk.

#### 1. Revenue Growth

Revenue is the largest single driver of profit growth and IRR in most acquisitions. Decompose it into three MECE buckets: volume, price/mix, and product/offering expansion. Each has a distinct evidence standard and diligence owner.

---

**1A. Volume / Units Sold**

*What it covers:* Growth in the number of customers, transactions, or units — independent of price changes.

**New customer acquisition**
Growth from winning customers who did not previously buy. Driven by sales force productivity, market demand, and go-to-market efficiency. At initial memo, estimate from historical revenue growth decomposed against visible pricing trends. CAC and pipeline quality are not available at screening and must be flagged as priority diligence items.

*Initial memo:* Decompose historical revenue growth into volume vs. price. Assess go-to-market model type (inbound vs. outbound, transactional vs. enterprise) as a structural proxy for CAC efficiency. Apply sector-norm customer growth rates conservatively.
*Full diligence:* Historical CAC by channel; quota attainment per sales rep for 3+ years; pipeline stage-gate conversion rates validated against historical bookings; win/loss analysis independent of management's CRM.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — channel strategy, CAC by channel, sales force productivity; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — segment sizing, buyer behavior, conversion triggers; **Dimension 2: Customer** — JTBD, segment prioritization, willingness to pay.

**Increased purchase frequency**
Existing customers buying more often — relevant for transactional, usage-based, or consumable business models. At initial memo, assess from business model type: subscription models have fixed frequency by design; transactional models have variable frequency that can expand.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model type and publicly disclosed usage or transaction data. Flag for validation if frequency expansion is a modeled driver.
*Full diligence:* Transactions per customer per period by cohort; frequency trend over customer tenure; customer interview findings on usage expansion appetite.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 8 (Customer Journey Mapping)** — engagement and loyalty stages, activation moments; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — buying behavior, trigger events.

**Expansion into new markets / geographies**
Revenue from customers or geographies the company does not currently serve. Highest-risk volume driver — include in base case only if the company has a demonstrated track record of successful expansion. Probability-weight at 50% of projected revenue if track record is absent.

*Initial memo:* Assess track record of prior geographic or vertical expansion and historical success rate. Flag as upside driver if track record is absent or limited.
*Full diligence:* Prior expansion initiative outcomes with revenue contribution and timeline; primary research with prospective customers in target market confirming willingness to pay; bottoms-up SAM in target geography with realistic penetration assumption.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 11 (Market Entry & Expansion)** — market attractiveness scoring, entry mode analysis, 12-month roadmap; **Prompt 1 (Market Sizing)** — TAM/SAM/SOM in target geography; **Dimension 1: Market** — structural trends, tailwinds, addressable market.

**Sales force productivity**
Growth from the existing sales team winning more, faster — through better tooling, better management, better incentives, or more targeted go-to-market. Distinct from headcount growth. At initial memo, assess from quota attainment history if disclosed; otherwise flag as a diligence item.

*Initial memo:* Assess go-to-market model structure and sales motion as a proxy for productivity improvement potential. Flag for validation.
*Full diligence:* Quota attainment by rep and by year; ramp time for new reps; pipeline velocity and conversion rates; sales manager span of control and coaching cadence.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — channel ROI, sales force structure, KPI framework; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — CAC by channel, LTV:CAC ratio, payback period.

---

**1B. Price / Mix**

*What it covers:* Growth from charging more per unit or shifting toward higher-value transactions — independent of volume changes.

**Market-level price increases**
Inflation-driven or sector-norm pricing growth. The baseline pricing assumption in any model — available at initial memo without company-specific data. Above-inflation pricing requires structural pricing power: low buyer concentration, high switching costs, no commodity pressure.

*Initial memo:* Establish from public comparable pricing history and sector structure. Inflation-level pricing (2–4%) is the default assumption. Above-inflation requires structural justification.
*Full diligence:* Realized price-per-unit history; public comparable pricing trends; Porter's Five Forces assessment of buyer power and competitive rivalry.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 5 (Porter's Five Forces)** — buyer power, competitive rivalry, threat of substitution; **Prompt 4 (Trend Analysis)** — macro forces, pricing environment; **Dimension 1: Market** — structural tailwinds and headwinds.

**Pricing realization above market**
Closing the gap between current realized pricing and the value delivered to customers — through list price increases, elimination of discounting, or contract restructuring at renewal. Distinct from market-level inflation. Include in base case only with specific evidence of underpricing; otherwise model as upside.

*Initial memo:* Flag as potential upside if sector analysis suggests structural underpricing. Do not include in base case without evidence.
*Full diligence:* Realized price per unit vs. list price vs. competitive benchmarks; discount frequency and depth by deal; pricing action history with volume and churn impact; customer interview findings on price sensitivity and switching cost.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — value-based pricing model, competitor pricing audit, price elasticity, discount strategy; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — willingness to pay, price sensitivity by segment; **Dimension 3: Economics** — gross margin, pricing power assessment.

**Reduced discounting**
Tightening discounting discipline to improve realized price without changing list price. Often the fastest-execution pricing lever with no product or market change required. Assess from discount frequency and depth in historical sales data.

*Initial memo:* Flag if the business model or sales motion (enterprise, direct sales) suggests discounting is common. Estimate magnitude from sector norms.
*Full diligence:* Average discount rate by deal size, channel, and sales rep; discount approval workflow; win/loss analysis on price-sensitive deals; customer interview findings on discount expectations.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — discount strategy and guardrails; **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — sales process and pricing authority.

**Premium product mix shift**
Revenue growth from customers moving to higher-priced tiers, products, or service levels. Requires a product architecture with natural upgrade paths and customers who have headroom to move up.

*Initial memo:* Assess from product architecture — does a premium tier exist and what is the current attach rate? Estimate mix shift potential from disclosed revenue breakdown if available.
*Full diligence:* Revenue and margin by product tier; historical mix trend; current attach rate vs. theoretical ceiling; customer interview findings on premium tier value perception and willingness to upgrade.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — 3-tier pricing recommendation, upsell monetization; **Prompt 8 (Customer Journey)** — loyalty and expansion stages; **Dimension 3: Economics** — revenue quality, margin by product.

**Upselling and cross-selling**
Revenue from selling additional products or services to existing customers. Highest-quality revenue driver — low CAC, high margin, high retention correlation. At initial memo, assess from product breadth and disclosed products-per-customer metrics if available.

*Initial memo:* Assess from number of products in the portfolio and any disclosed attach rate or multi-product penetration data. Apply conservative sector-norm expansion assumption.
*Full diligence:* NRR by expansion mechanism; current attach rate by product by customer segment; historical conversion rate from single- to multi-product; customer interview findings on adjacent product awareness and appetite.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — cross-sell and upsell monetization opportunities; **Prompt 8 (Customer Journey)** — engagement and loyalty stages; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — LTV expansion mechanics.

**Channel mix shift**
Revenue growth from shifting volume toward higher-margin or faster-growing channels — for example, DTC vs. wholesale, direct vs. reseller, digital vs. physical. Assess from current channel revenue breakdown and margin differential by channel.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model type and any disclosed channel breakdown. Flag if channel mix is a material model assumption.
*Full diligence:* Revenue, margin, and growth rate by channel; customer acquisition dynamics by channel; strategic rationale and execution risk for channel shift; competitive dynamics by channel.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — channel strategy and ROI ranking; **Prompt 2 (Competitive Landscape)** — competitive positioning by channel; **Dimension 4: Competition** — channel-level competitive dynamics.

---

**1C. Product / Offering Expansion**

*What it covers:* Revenue from capabilities that do not yet exist — new products, services, or bundles. Highest-risk revenue driver category. Always probability-weighted; never modeled at full value without demonstrated track record.

**New product launches**
Revenue from products that are in development or not yet released. Include in base case only if a specific product is in late-stage development with a defined launch timeline and customer validation. Otherwise upside scenario only.

*Initial memo:* Assess from management presentations or CIM — is a new product named, scoped, and validated? Flag as upside if not.
*Full diligence:* Development stage and timeline; customer validation (LOIs, beta users, pilot revenue); comparable product launch timelines and ramp rates; competitive context for the new product at launch.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 1 (Market Sizing)** — TAM for new product; **Prompt 2 (Competitive Landscape)** — competitive position for new product; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — target customer for new product, willingness to pay.

**New services or bundles**
Revenue from packaging existing capabilities differently — bundles, managed services, professional services, or outcome-based pricing. Lower risk than new product launches because the underlying capability exists; the risk is commercial, not technical.

*Initial memo:* Assess from current offering architecture — does unbundled capability exist that could be repackaged? Estimate based on comparable bundling economics in the sector.
*Full diligence:* Customer interview findings on bundled offering appetite; pricing analysis for bundled vs. unbundled; margin impact of bundling; competitive response risk.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — bundling and monetization; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — JTBD for bundled offering; **Dimension 3: Economics** — margin impact of service mix.

**Innovation driving higher willingness to pay**
Revenue uplift from product improvements that allow the company to charge more without increasing volume. Requires validated evidence of customer willingness to pay for the improvement — not a product roadmap.

*Initial memo:* Flag as upside driver if R&D investment is evident and innovation track record is strong. Do not model without evidence.
*Full diligence:* Customer interview findings on specific feature value and WTP; pricing analysis for enhanced vs. base product; historical evidence of innovation-driven price realization; R&D investment trajectory vs. revenue impact.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — value-based pricing model; **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — willingness to pay, functional and emotional JTBD; **Dimension 5: Capability** — innovation capability assessment.

#### 2. Gross Margin Expansion

Gross margin improvement compounds directly with revenue growth — a business growing 15% with expanding gross margin creates significantly more EBITDA than one growing 15% at flat gross margins. Decompose into COGS reduction and mix effects.

---

**2A. COGS Reduction**

*What it covers:* Reducing the direct cost of delivering each unit of revenue.

**Supplier / input cost reduction through renegotiation**
Negotiating better terms with existing suppliers — lower unit cost, better payment terms, volume commitments in exchange for pricing concessions. Most credible for businesses where a single supplier category represents a meaningful share of COGS and where volume growth creates leverage.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model — does the company have significant third-party input costs that scale with revenue? Is the company below the volume threshold at which meaningful supplier leverage is available?
*Full diligence:* Current supplier contracts and pricing vs. market benchmarks; supplier concentration; historical renegotiation outcomes; volume growth required to unlock next pricing tier.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — gross margin per unit, cost structure; **Dimension 3: Economics** — key margin lever, cost structure assessment; **Prompt 5 (Porter's Five Forces)** — supplier power rating.

**Manufacturing or operational efficiency**
Reducing the labor, material, or overhead cost per unit through process improvement — without capital investment. Credible for businesses with identifiable inefficiencies in production or service delivery. Requires operational diligence, not commercial diligence.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model type and gross margin vs. sector benchmarks. A gross margin materially below sector peers suggests operational inefficiency or structural cost disadvantage — determine which.
*Full diligence:* Gross margin vs. sector benchmarks with explanation of gap; unit cost trend over time; capacity utilization and throughput analysis; operational benchmarking vs. comparable facilities or service operations.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — gross margin per unit/customer, contribution margin; **Dimension 3: Economics** — gross margin, key margin lever; **Prompt 5 (SWOT)** — operational weaknesses.

**Automation and yield improvement**
Capital investment that replaces variable labor cost or improves output per unit of input. Consistently overestimated — apply a haircut of 50% to projected savings at 150% of projected timeline unless a specific initiative is underway with demonstrated progress.

*Initial memo:* Flag as upside driver unless a specific initiative is scoped, funded, and underway. Do not include in base case without progress evidence.
*Full diligence:* Specific automation initiatives with investment cost, labor reduction, and timeline; comparable technology investment outcomes in similar operations; management's track record with technology-led efficiency programs; implementation risk assessment.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Dimension 5: Capability** — build/buy/partner for automation capability; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure, fixed vs. variable; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — operational risk of technology implementation.

**Logistics optimization**
Reducing inbound or outbound logistics cost through network redesign, carrier renegotiation, or route optimization. Relevant for businesses with significant physical distribution. Assess from logistics as a share of COGS and current network structure.

*Initial memo:* Assess materiality of logistics in cost structure from business model type. Flag if logistics represents more than 10% of COGS.
*Full diligence:* Logistics cost as a share of COGS by channel and geography; carrier contract benchmarking; network design vs. customer geography; comparable logistics optimization outcomes.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure decomposition; **Dimension 3: Economics** — key margin lever; **Prompt 5 (Porter's Five Forces)** — supplier power (carrier concentration).

**2B. Mix Effects on Gross Margin**

*What it covers:* Improving blended gross margin by shifting the composition of revenue toward higher-margin products, customers, or channels — without changing the unit economics of any individual offering.

**Higher-margin product or service mix**
If the business has products with materially different gross margins, growth in higher-margin products improves blended gross margin without any cost reduction. Assess from product-level margin disclosure and historical mix trend.

*Initial memo:* Assess from disclosed product revenue breakdown and comparable business margin profiles by product type. If product-level margins are undisclosed, flag as a priority diligence request.
*Full diligence:* Gross margin by product line; historical mix trend with explanation; go-to-market actions required to accelerate high-margin mix; customer-level margin by segment.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — gross margin per product/customer; **Dimension 3: Economics** — revenue quality, margin by product; **Prompt 6 (Pricing Strategy)** — product tier margin architecture.

**Favorable customer mix**
Higher-margin customers (typically enterprise, high-volume, or long-tenure) growing faster than lower-margin customers improves blended gross margin. Assess from customer-level margin data and cohort growth trends.

*Initial memo:* Assess from disclosed customer segmentation and any available cohort data. Flag for validation if customer mix is a meaningful assumption.
*Full diligence:* Gross margin by customer tier and cohort vintage; growth rate of high-margin vs. low-margin segments; churn rate differential by segment; customer interview findings on value-to-price ratio by segment.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 3 (Customer Personas)** — segment sizing and prioritization; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — gross margin per customer; **Dimension 2: Customer** — segment prioritization, willingness to pay.

**Shift to digital or lower variable cost delivery**
Moving delivery of a product or service from high-variable-cost to low-variable-cost infrastructure — for example, from in-person to digital, from physical to software, or from custom to standardized. This is a structural margin improvement that is durable once achieved but typically requires upfront investment and carries customer adoption risk.

*Initial memo:* Assess from current delivery model and sector trend toward digital delivery. Flag as a potential structural margin driver if the delivery model is transitioning.
*Full diligence:* Variable cost per delivery method; customer adoption rate of digital delivery; investment required and timeline to shift; competitive context — are competitors already digital-first?
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 4 (Trend Analysis)** — technology disruption timeline; **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — channel strategy; **Dimension 5: Capability** — build vs. buy for digital delivery capability.

#### 3. Operating Expense (OpEx) Leverage

OpEx leverage is the compounding effect of revenue growing faster than the cost base required to support it. It is the most predictable margin driver for businesses with predominantly fixed or semi-fixed cost structures — and the most overestimated for businesses with variable cost structures.

---

**3A. Fixed Cost Leverage**

**Revenue growing faster than fixed costs**
The structural margin improvement that occurs when fixed overhead — rent, corporate infrastructure, management team, technology platforms — does not scale proportionally with revenue. This is the most defensible OpEx leverage driver because it requires no management action; it occurs automatically if revenue grows.

*Initial memo:* Estimate from business model type. Asset-light software and services businesses have high fixed cost ratios and strong leverage. Businesses with variable labor (e.g., staffing, transactional services) have low fixed cost ratios and limited leverage. Apply 50–100bps of margin improvement per 10% revenue growth as a conservative default for fixed-cost-dominant businesses.
*Full diligence:* Cost structure decomposed into fixed vs. variable by line item; historical OpEx as a percentage of revenue vs. revenue growth with explanation of any divergence; comparable public company OpEx leverage at similar revenue scales.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure, fixed vs. variable, break-even analysis; **Dimension 3: Economics** — key margin lever, operational leverage assessment.

**Better utilization of existing infrastructure**
Increasing throughput from infrastructure already paid for — server capacity, warehouse space, production lines, licensed software. The cost is already incurred; additional volume flows through at near-zero marginal cost. Assess from current utilization rates and capacity headroom.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model — does the company have significant fixed infrastructure with headroom for volume growth? Flag for validation if infrastructure utilization is a meaningful margin driver.
*Full diligence:* Current utilization rates by infrastructure category; incremental cost to add volume within existing capacity; capacity expansion trigger point and investment required.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — contribution margin, break-even; **Dimension 5: Capability** — operational capacity assessment.

---

**3B. SG&A Efficiency**

**Sales and marketing efficiency (CAC reduction, ROI improvement)**
Improving the return on sales and marketing investment — acquiring the same customers at lower cost, or better customers at the same cost. Distinct from fixed cost leverage because sales and marketing cost is often partially variable. Assess from historical CAC trend and marketing channel ROI.

*Initial memo:* Assess from go-to-market model type. Inbound and product-led models have structurally lower CAC than outbound enterprise sales. Flag for validation if sales efficiency improvement is a modeled margin driver.
*Full diligence:* CAC by channel with trend; marketing ROI by channel; quota attainment history; sales cycle length and conversion rates; payback period by cohort.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — CAC by channel, budget allocation, ROI; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — CAC, LTV:CAC ratio, payback period; **Dimension 3: Economics** — unit economics assessment.

**G&A cost control**
Reducing or holding flat finance, HR, legal, and administrative overhead as revenue scales. Credible as a fixed cost leverage driver but should not be double-counted with the fixed cost leverage section above. Specific G&A reduction initiatives (outsourcing, system automation, headcount rationalization) belong here.

*Initial memo:* Assess G&A as a percentage of revenue vs. sector benchmarks for companies at comparable scale. Material above-benchmark G&A suggests either inefficiency or dis-synergy from the current ownership structure.
*Full diligence:* G&A breakdown by function; G&A as a percentage of revenue vs. sector benchmarks and comparable companies at similar scale; specific reduction opportunities with dollar amounts and implementation costs.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure, G&A benchmark; **Prompt 5 (SWOT)** — operational weaknesses; **Dimension 3: Economics** — key margin lever.

**Headcount optimization**
Reducing labor cost through restructuring, role elimination, or offshoring — without impairing revenue. A one-time margin improvement, not a sustainable growth lever. Model the one-time restructuring cost against the run-rate saving and confirm the saving is achievable without revenue impact.

*Initial memo:* Flag if headcount appears above sector norms for the revenue base. Do not model without specific initiative identified.
*Full diligence:* Headcount by function vs. sector benchmarks; specific roles or layers identified for elimination; restructuring cost estimate; risk assessment of revenue impact from headcount reduction.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 5 (SWOT)** — organizational weaknesses; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure; **Dimension 5: Capability** — capability gap analysis, build vs. buy.

---

**3C. Marketing Efficiency**

**Lower CAC through better targeting and conversion**
Reducing the cost per acquired customer through improved lead quality, better targeting, higher conversion rates, or channel mix shift toward lower-cost channels. Related to but distinct from overall sales and marketing efficiency — this is specifically about the acquisition funnel.

*Initial memo:* Assess from go-to-market model. Businesses with high inbound content marketing or product-led growth have structurally improving CAC as brand and SEO compound. Flag for validation.
*Full diligence:* CAC trend by channel over 3+ years; conversion rates by funnel stage; channel mix shift and ROI differential; customer interview findings on discovery channel.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — acquisition channel ROI, conversion rates; **Prompt 8 (Customer Journey)** — awareness and consideration stages; **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — CAC by channel, payback period.

**Higher LTV through retention and expansion**
Improving the lifetime value of each acquired customer — through longer retention, higher expansion, or both. This is the demand-side complement to CAC reduction. A business improving both CAC and LTV simultaneously improves the LTV:CAC ratio, which is the single best indicator of unit economics health.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model retention characteristics and product expansion architecture. Flag for validation.
*Full diligence:* LTV by cohort by acquisition channel; retention curve by vintage; expansion revenue per customer by tenure; NRR trend; customer interview findings on long-term value and churn risk.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — LTV calculation, LTV:CAC ratio; **Prompt 8 (Customer Journey)** — loyalty and churn stages; **Dimension 3: Economics** — unit economics, payback period.

#### 4. Operating Model Improvements

Structural changes to how the business operates that improve profitability without being reducible to a single cost line. These are cross-cutting initiatives that affect multiple P&L lines simultaneously and typically require 12–24 months to realize.

**Automation and digitization**
Technology investment that replaces manual processes across the business — not just in COGS (covered above) but across fulfillment, customer service, finance, and operations. Model the full investment required, the full run-rate saving, and the realistic timeline. Apply a 50% haircut to management's saving estimate and 150% of the projected timeline as conservative defaults.

*Initial memo:* Flag as upside unless a specific initiative is underway. Do not include in base case without evidence of scoped initiative and demonstrated progress.
*Full diligence:* Current process automation state vs. sector benchmarks; specific initiative scope, investment, and timeline; labor cost reduction achievable; comparable outcomes in similar businesses; management technology implementation track record.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Dimension 5: Capability** — capability gap, build vs. buy; **Prompt 4 (Trend Analysis)** — technology disruption; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — operational implementation risk.

**Outsourcing vs. in-house optimization**
Shifting from in-house delivery of non-core functions to outsourced providers (or vice versa, where insourcing reduces cost). Most credible for functions where the company is sub-scale relative to specialist providers — finance, IT, HR, customer support, logistics.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model and current organizational structure. Flag if the company appears to be running functions in-house that are typically outsourced at this scale.
*Full diligence:* Function-by-function cost benchmarking vs. outsourced alternatives; transition cost and timeline; quality and control risk of outsourcing; vendor market assessment.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure; **Dimension 5: Capability** — build vs. buy vs. partner; **Prompt 5 (SWOT)** — operational weaknesses.

**Process redesign (lean operations)**
Eliminating waste, reducing cycle time, and improving throughput through process improvement without capital investment. Typically a management-execution initiative rather than a structural change. Credible if the management team has a demonstrated lean or operational excellence track record; less credible if it is a generic assumption.

*Initial memo:* Flag as potential upside if management team has demonstrated operational improvement track record. Do not model without evidence.
*Full diligence:* Management team operational improvement track record with outcomes; current process benchmarking vs. best practice; specific initiatives identified with saving potential; implementation risk.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 5 (SWOT)** — operational strengths and weaknesses; **Dimension 5: Capability** — operational capability assessment; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — execution risk.

**Supply chain redesign**
Structural reconfiguration of the supply chain — sourcing geography, supplier base, inventory positioning, or distribution network — to reduce cost or improve resilience. A multi-year initiative with significant upfront investment. Include in base case only if the initiative is already underway or if a specific supply chain opportunity has been identified in diligence.

*Initial memo:* Flag if supply chain concentration or cost appears above sector norms. Do not model as a base case driver without specific initiative.
*Full diligence:* Supply chain cost and structure vs. sector benchmarks; specific redesign initiative with investment, saving, and timeline; supplier concentration and transition risk; customer impact during transition.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 5 (Porter's Five Forces)** — supplier power; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — supply chain risk; **Dimension 3: Economics** — gross margin and key cost drivers.

---

#### 5. Scale Effects

Scale effects are structural — they improve automatically as the business grows, without specific management initiatives. They should be modeled as a baseline assumption in any growing business, not as a value creation initiative. Over-attributing scale effects to management skill double-counts the revenue growth contribution.

**Purchasing power and volume discounts**
As revenue grows, the company's spend with suppliers grows proportionally, unlocking better pricing tiers. Model as a COGS improvement assumption tied directly to the revenue growth rate, not as a separate management initiative.

*Initial memo:* Estimate from business model — relevant for businesses with significant third-party input costs that scale with revenue. Apply conservatively based on sector procurement benchmarks.
*Full diligence:* Current supplier pricing vs. next-tier benchmarks; volume required to unlock next pricing tier; procurement track record and supplier relationship quality.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure, gross margin; **Prompt 5 (Porter's Five Forces)** — supplier power; **Dimension 3: Economics** — key margin lever.

**Fixed cost spreading across larger revenue base**
Covered under 3A (Fixed Cost Leverage). Do not model separately — double-counting fixed cost leverage and scale effects inflates the margin expansion assumption.

**Brand strength reducing marketing cost per unit**
As the brand compounds — through earned media, word of mouth, and category leadership — CAC declines and inbound lead quality improves without incremental marketing investment. A real effect in brand-driven businesses; less relevant in B2B enterprise or commoditized markets.

*Initial memo:* Assess from business model and brand position. Relevant for consumer-facing, community-driven, or category-defining B2B businesses. Less relevant for enterprise or transactional models.
*Full diligence:* Inbound vs. outbound lead mix trend; CAC trend over time controlling for marketing spend; NPS and brand awareness metrics; customer interview findings on how they discovered the product.
*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 7 (GTM Strategy)** — channel mix and CAC; **Prompt 2 (Competitive Landscape)** — brand moat assessment; **Dimension 4: Competition** — brand as a competitive moat.

---

#### 6. One-Time and Non-Recurring Factors

Model these separately from recurring profit drivers. They improve EBITDA in the period they occur but do not sustain — and a buyer at exit will normalize them out of the earnings base. Include them in the return model as a one-time cash flow benefit, not as a multiple-bearing EBITDA contribution.

**Cost-cutting programs and restructuring**
One-time reduction in the cost base through headcount reduction, facility consolidation, or contract termination. Model the one-time restructuring charge against the run-rate saving. The saving is recurring; the charge is not. Assess the risk that restructuring impairs revenue (customer-facing headcount) or culture (morale impact on remaining employees).

*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 9 (Unit Economics)** — cost structure; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — operational and reputational risk of restructuring.

**Temporary input cost tailwinds**
Commodity, energy, or labor cost reductions that reflect market conditions rather than management action. Do not model as a sustainable margin driver. If the current cost environment is favorable, assess the downside scenario where it normalizes and model the margin impact.

*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 4 (Trend Analysis)** — macro forces, input cost environment; **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — market risk, input cost reversal.

**FX impacts**
For businesses with international revenue or costs, currency movements create non-operational P&L impact. Model on a constant-currency basis for operational drivers; model FX separately as a scenario variable. Do not include favorable FX in the base case unless the business has a natural hedge.

*`mckinsey-consultant` workstream:* **Prompt 10 (Risk Assessment)** — financial risk, FX exposure; **Prompt 4 (Trend Analysis)** — macroeconomic forces.

#### Reconciliation and Integrity Check

After building the return waterfall with granular drivers, apply three tests before accepting the model as the basis for IC approval.

**Test 1 — Evidence tier audit:** For every driver contributing more than 5% of total IRR, identify the evidence tier: market data (available at screening), company historical data (requires data room), or primary research (requires customer and channel interviews). Every driver supported only by initial memo estimates must be flagged as an assumption in the diligence workplan. A driver that remains unvalidated at IC is an unpriced risk, not a base case assumption.

**Test 2 — Asymmetry check:** Build a downside scenario where every driver not validated by primary research performs at sector norm rather than company-specific levels. If the downside scenario does not return capital, the thesis is more speculative than the base case suggests.

**Test 3 — Independence and double-count check:** Confirm that scale effects, fixed cost leverage, and specific management initiatives are not overlapping. Revenue growth assumptions that depend on both volume and pricing from the same customer base are partially correlated. Operating leverage and pricing realization may be inversely correlated — achieving pricing growth may require accepting lower volume. One-time factors must not be capitalized into the exit multiple. Where drivers are correlated or overlapping, the base case is overstated and must be stress-tested with a correlated downside.

**Red flags:** Multiple expansion above 30% of total IRR without a specific value-creation rationale; revenue growth not disaggregated by driver and therefore not linkable to diligence workstreams; one-time cost savings included in the multiple-bearing EBITDA base; technology-driven margin improvement modeled at management's estimate without a haircut; initial memo assumptions carried through to IC without replacement by diligence findings; `mckinsey-consultant` workstreams assigned but not executed before IC.

### 3.5 Value Creation Roadmap: PE-Specific Levers

**What to establish:** The specific initiatives that PE ownership enables — and that would not occur, or would occur more slowly, without sponsor involvement. The value creation roadmap must be more than "professionalize operations and grow." Each initiative must be specific, sized, timed, and conditioned.

Map each value creation initiative across four categories:

**Category A — Revenue acceleration:** Specific initiatives to accelerate top-line growth that PE ownership enables. Examples: sales force build-out with a defined quota-carrying headcount plan; new vertical or geographic expansion with a named market entry playbook; pricing realization through a specific repricing event or contract renegotiation cycle; partnership or channel development with named targets and an expected revenue contribution timeline.

**Category B — Margin improvement:** Specific cost reduction or efficiency initiatives. Examples: procurement renegotiation with a quantified saving potential; shared services centralization with a defined implementation plan and cost reduction target; technology investment that replaces labor cost with a specific payback period; management of unprofitable customer relationships with a churn-versus-retain decision framework.

**Category C — Strategic repositioning:** Initiatives that change the asset's market position or revenue quality. Examples: product investment that moves the company toward a recurring revenue model; M&A tuck-ins that add capability or market access, with a named pipeline and integration playbook; brand investment that expands the addressable buyer universe or improves pricing power.

**Category D — Balance sheet and capital structure optimization:** Initiatives to improve capital efficiency. Examples: working capital improvement with a specific DSO/DPO target; sale-leaseback of owned real estate; optimization of the debt structure to reduce interest cost or extend maturity; dividend recapitalization timing relative to EBITDA milestones.

For each initiative: size the EBITDA impact (base and upside case), assign a timeline (year of implementation and year of full run-rate impact), identify the required investment (capex, management bandwidth, external advisory), and name the risk that prevents capture.

**Red flags:** A value creation roadmap where all initiatives are back-weighted to years 3–5 with no near-term proof points; initiatives that depend on management executing a strategy they have already attempted and failed to execute independently; "professionalize the back office" as the primary margin lever — which signals the asset is being acquired for operational fix-up rather than strategic value creation; a roadmap where every initiative is additive and none involves trade-offs or resource prioritization, which signals it has not been stress-tested against management bandwidth or capital constraints.

---

### 3.6 Timing of Value Capture

**What to establish:** When the value creation occurs within the hold period — and whether the return profile is front-loaded, back-weighted, or evenly distributed. The timing of value creation has direct implications for IRR sensitivity and the sequencing of capital decisions.

- Map the expected EBITDA profile by year across the hold period. In what year does each value creation initiative reach full run-rate impact? Is the EBITDA trajectory front-loaded (early operational improvements and quick wins), back-weighted (requires scale or M&A to realize), or evenly distributed?
- Assess the cash flow profile relative to the debt service schedule. A business with back-weighted EBITDA growth but front-weighted debt service requirements may face a liquidity stress in years 2–3 even if the long-run thesis is intact. Map the free cash flow after debt service by year explicitly.
- Identify the earliest credible exit window. This is not necessarily the target exit date — it is the earliest point at which the asset has realized enough of the value creation thesis to attract a buyer at an exit multiple consistent with the return target. An early exit option has option value even if it is not the plan.
- Assess IRR sensitivity to hold period. Calculate IRR at exit years 3, 5, and 7. If IRR degrades sharply with a 1-year extension of the hold, the thesis is fragile. A resilient thesis should show reasonable IRR across a range of hold periods, with the primary sensitivity being the exit multiple rather than the timing.

---

### 3.7 Risk-Adjusted Return Assessment

**What to establish:** Whether the return is attractive relative to the risks taken — not just in absolute terms. An IRR of 25% in a low-risk, defensible asset with a predictable path is a better risk-adjusted return than 25% in a high-execution, high-dependency thesis.

- Build three explicit scenarios — base, upside, and downside — with specific assumptions for each, not just a percentage adjustment to the base case. The downside scenario should reflect a realistic, coherent adverse narrative (sector contraction, key customer loss, competitive disruption, macro deterioration) rather than a generic haircut.
- Calculate IRR and MOIC for each scenario. Assess whether the downside scenario impairs the return of capital or merely the return on capital — the former is a loss-of-principal scenario; the latter is a below-target return.
- Apply a risk-weighting to the three scenarios (e.g., 20% upside / 60% base / 20% downside) and calculate the probability-weighted expected return. Compare this to the hurdle rate and to the expected return of alternative uses of the same capital.
- Assess the specific risks that are most likely to impair returns: identify the top 3 risks, rate each on probability and impact, and for each, define the early warning indicator and the mitigation action. The point is not to eliminate risk — it is to ensure that the return is sufficient compensation for the risks that are being accepted knowingly.

---

### 3.8 Entry Valuation Assessment

**What to establish:** Whether the entry price is fair, attractive, or demanding relative to the company's fundamental quality and the expected return — and whether there is a margin of safety in the valuation.

- Triangulate entry valuation across at least three methods: comparable company trading multiples (public peers, adjusted for liquidity discount and size); precedent transactions in the sector (recent M&A, adjusted for deal structure, strategic premium, and market conditions at time of transaction); and intrinsic value via discounted cash flow using conservative assumptions.
- Assess the implied growth rate embedded in the entry valuation. What revenue and EBITDA growth rate does the entry price assume over the hold period? Is that assumption consistent with the historical track record, the sector growth rate, and the specific value creation initiatives identified?
- Identify the valuation risk: under what circumstances does the entry price prove to have been too high? Specifically: what EBITDA trajectory would cause the return to fall below the hurdle rate at the target exit multiple? This defines the downside protection threshold.
- Assess competitive dynamics in the deal process. Was this a competitive auction or a proprietary process? In a competitive auction, the entry price reflects the most optimistic buyer's valuation — which means the buyer who wins has implicitly bet they are more capable of realizing value than every other sophisticated buyer in the process. Acknowledge this and ensure the value creation thesis supports the premium paid.

---

### 3.9 Capital Structure and Leverage Assessment

**What to establish:** That the capital structure is appropriate for the business's cash flow profile and risk level — and that leverage enhances returns without creating unacceptable downside risk.

- Calculate leverage at entry (Debt/EBITDA) and compare to: the sector norm for this type of asset; the company's demonstrated cash conversion rate (what percentage of EBITDA converts to free cash flow available for debt service?); and the downside scenario EBITDA (at what leverage ratio does the business operate in the downside case?).
- Assess the debt service coverage ratio in the base case and downside case. A DSCR of 1.2x in the base case and 0.8x in the downside case is a fundamentally different risk profile from a DSCR of 2.0x in the base case and 1.4x in the downside case.
- Review covenant package. What are the maintenance covenants, at what level are they set, and what is the headroom in the base case and downside case? Covenant breaches can force a restructuring at precisely the moment when a patient hold would generate a better outcome.
- Assess refinancing risk. When does the debt mature, and what is the refinancing assumption? A business that requires refinancing in year 3 of a 5-year hold is exposed to the credit market environment in year 3 — which may or may not be favorable. Size the refinancing risk explicitly.

## Question 4: Why Will We Exit at the Assumed Price?

*The standard: a credible, specific exit thesis — not a hope that multiples hold or that a buyer emerges. The buyer universe is named, the exit multiple is underwritten against a specific buyer's logic, the asset's readiness for exit is assessed honestly, and the return is stress-tested against the scenarios where the exit thesis is wrong.*

---

### 4.1 Buyer Universe and Exit Path

**What to establish:** Who will buy this asset at exit, at what price, and why — with specificity about the buyer's strategic rationale and what the asset needs to look like at the time of sale to attract that buyer at the assumed multiple.

- Name the realistic buyer universe across all three exit paths: strategic acquirer (name the top 5 companies that would pay a premium and state their acquisition rationale — what capability gap does this asset fill, what synergy does it create, and why would they pay above market to own it rather than build or partner?); sponsor-to-sponsor (identify the 3–5 PE firms that have invested in this sector at this scale, and assess whether their fund vintage and deployment timeline make them active buyers in years 3–7 of your hold); and public market / IPO (at what scale, margin profile, and revenue quality does this asset become an IPO candidate, and is that scenario realistic within the hold period?).
- For each buyer category, assess the probability-weighted likelihood of execution. A buyer universe that is theoretically possible but requires a specific strategic event (a competitor needing to respond to disruption, a consolidator in accumulation mode) is a lower-confidence exit than one where multiple financial buyers would acquire this asset as a standard portfolio addition.
- Assess whether the buyer universe is expanding or contracting over the hold period. A sector that is consolidating and where strategic acquirers are actively deploying capital today may have fewer acquirers at exit if consolidation has run its course. Model the buyer universe at exit, not just today.
- Identify the minimum asset profile required to attract the assumed buyer at the assumed multiple: what revenue scale, EBITDA margin, retention profile, and geographic footprint does the asset need to achieve? Map this against the base case value creation plan and assess whether it is achievable within the hold period.

**Red flags:** An exit thesis that names a single strategic acquirer with no fallback — if that buyer is not active at exit, the entire exit thesis fails; a buyer universe that has narrowed materially since entry (consolidation complete, regulatory change, strategic pivot at named acquirers); an assumed exit multiple that is above the current entry multiple for comparable assets without a specific value creation rationale for the premium.

---

### 4.2 Exit Multiple Sustainability

**What to establish:** Whether the exit multiple assumed in the return model is achievable — underwritten against a specific buyer's valuation logic, a specific asset profile, and a specific market environment — not simply assumed to hold at entry levels.

- Underwrite the exit multiple from the buyer's perspective, not the seller's. A strategic acquirer pays a multiple based on the synergies they can extract — not the asset's standalone earnings. A financial buyer pays a multiple based on the return they need to generate for their LPs at their expected hold period and exit. For each buyer type, build the reverse DCF: what multiple can they afford to pay and still hit their return target? If the assumed exit multiple requires the buyer to accept below-market returns, it is not achievable.
- Assess sector multiple trajectory. Is the current entry multiple above, at, or below the 10-year historical average for this asset category? If above: what sustains it at or above that level through the hold period? If current multiples are elevated relative to history, the base case should underwrite exit at current multiples as the ceiling, not the floor.
- Identify the conditions under which the exit multiple compresses materially. The top three risks to multiple sustainability are typically: sector re-rating (market-wide derating of the category due to rate environment, competitive disruption, or narrative shift); asset-specific deterioration (revenue quality decline, margin erosion, or customer concentration increase that moves the asset into a lower-multiple peer group); and market timing (forced exit in an adverse credit or equity environment). For each, assess probability and the return outcome if it materializes.
- Assess whether there is a scale or quality threshold that unlocks a meaningfully higher multiple — and whether the value creation plan achieves it. Some assets trade at a significant multiple premium above a specific revenue or EBITDA threshold (e.g., above $50M EBITDA, the buyer universe expands to include large-cap PE, which pays higher multiples). If the value creation plan gets the asset to that threshold, model the multiple step-up explicitly.

**Red flags:** Exit multiple assumed at or above entry multiple with no specific rationale for why the buyer universe is deeper or more competitive at exit than at entry; multiple expansion as the largest single contributor to IRR — a thesis where the return is primarily a function of market conditions rather than value creation is fragile; no explicit modeling of a multiple compression scenario in the return analysis.

---

### 4.3 Exit Readiness and Asset Preparation

**What to establish:** That the asset will be in a condition to run a competitive exit process at the target exit date — with the financial profile, management team, operational infrastructure, and data room quality that sophisticated buyers require.

- Assess the current state of the asset's financial reporting infrastructure. Can the company produce GAAP-quality audited financials, clean EBITDA reconciliations, and cohort-level KPI data that a sophisticated buyer's diligence team will require? If not, identify the investment and timeline required to get there — and build it into the value creation plan as a prerequisite, not an afterthought.
- Evaluate management team continuity and equity alignment through exit. Is the current management team — or the team that will be in place at exit — incentivized to cooperate with and support a sale process? Are there equity plan cliff dates, retention agreements, or personal succession plans that create misalignment between management's interests and the timing of an optimal exit? Map these explicitly and address them in the management equity package at entry.
- Assess whether there are structural obstacles to a clean exit: minority shareholder rights, customer contract change-of-control provisions, regulatory approvals required at exit, earn-out obligations from prior acquisitions, or litigation exposure that would create a price chip or transaction delay. Identify each obstacle and the path to resolution before the exit window.
- Identify the 3–5 specific actions required to maximize exit value that are not core to the operating business — the narrative preparation, the EBITDA normalization documentation, the customer reference program, the data room build. Assign ownership and timeline for each. An exit that is prepared from day one of ownership consistently achieves better outcomes than one that is assembled reactively when a buyer emerges.

**Red flags:** A management team with no equity or below-market equity whose cooperation with a sale process cannot be assumed; material customer contracts with change-of-control provisions that require consent of the counterparty — which creates negotiating leverage for the customer at the worst possible time; a financial reporting infrastructure that produces management accounts but not GAAP-quality audited financials, which creates a credibility discount in any buyer's diligence.

---

### 4.4 Process Optionality and Timing Flexibility

**What to establish:** That the return is not dependent on executing a specific exit in a specific window — and that there is genuine optionality in the timing, form, and counterparty of the exit.

- Assess the range of exit forms available: full sale, partial recapitalization (sell a majority stake and roll equity), dividend recapitalization (extract capital while retaining ownership), IPO, or continuation fund. Each has different return characteristics, different timing requirements, and different market conditions under which it is executable. A thesis that is dependent on a single exit form in a specific market environment is more fragile than one with multiple paths to liquidity.
- Evaluate the hold period flexibility. What is the minimum hold period at which an acceptable return is achievable in the base case? What is the maximum hold period before the thesis begins to degrade — either because value creation initiatives have run their course, because leverage becomes a drag rather than an enhancer, or because the asset ages out of the optimal buyer universe? Define the rational hold range and assess whether it provides adequate flexibility.
- Map the optimal exit timing relative to the sector cycle. In cyclical sectors, the exit multiple varies significantly across the cycle — and the difference between exiting at peak versus trough can dwarf the impact of the operating value creation. Identify where in the sector cycle the exit is targeted, and assess what happens to the return if the cycle is 18 months out of phase with the plan.
- Assess the option value of an early exit. If the value creation thesis delivers its primary initiatives in years 1–3, is there a credible buyer at year 3 who would pay an exit multiple consistent with the return target? Early exit optionality — even if not the plan — has real value because it creates negotiating leverage and provides a floor on the return distribution.

**Red flags:** A return model that is only analyzed at a single exit year with no sensitivity to hold period extension; a thesis that requires a full-auction exit process in a specific 12-month window with no alternative liquidity path; no explicit analysis of what happens to the return if the primary strategic acquirer is not active at the planned exit date.

---

## Question 5: Why Are We the Right Owner?

*The standard: a specific, evidenced answer to why Pattern creates more value in this asset than any other buyer — and what it would cost to lose this deal to a competitor. If the answer is "we paid the right price," that is a financial buyer answer. A strategic buyer must be able to articulate the incremental value only Pattern can create, the specific capabilities Pattern brings that the asset cannot develop independently, and the concrete actions Pattern will take in the first 100 days that a financial buyer would not.*

---

### 5.1 Strategic Fit with Pattern's Platform

**What to establish:** The specific ways this acquisition strengthens Pattern's existing platform — not in abstract terms ("expands our addressable market") but in terms of named capabilities added, named customer relationships deepened, named cost structures improved, and named competitive positions strengthened.

- Map the acquisition against Pattern's existing business across four dimensions: capability (what does this asset do that Pattern currently cannot do, or does poorly, or does at higher cost?); customer (what customer relationships does this asset bring that Pattern can now deepen, cross-sell into, or retain more durably?); data (what data assets does this acquisition add, and how do those data assets improve Pattern's existing product decisions, pricing models, or customer targeting?); and infrastructure (what operational infrastructure — fulfillment, technology, people — does this acquisition add that reduces Pattern's cost per unit of service delivered or expands Pattern's addressable capacity?).
- Quantify the synergy potential across revenue and cost dimensions. Revenue synergies: which of Pattern's existing customers would adopt the acquired asset's products, at what revenue per customer, within what timeline? Which of the acquired asset's customers would adopt Pattern's existing products, with the same specificity? Cost synergies: which overhead functions can be consolidated, at what dollar savings, within what timeline and at what one-time cost? Apply a discount to stated synergy estimates — revenue synergies typically take twice as long and realize at half the rate projected at acquisition.
- Assess fit with Pattern's strategic direction. Is this acquisition consistent with where Pattern is investing for the next 5 years, or does it represent a diversification that requires Pattern to develop competence in a new domain? Acquisitions that extend an existing capability are structurally easier to integrate and more likely to realize synergies than acquisitions that expand into new markets or business models.
- Identify the specific Pattern executive or operating team who will own this integration — not a committee, not a to-be-hired hire. If there is not a named person with the bandwidth, domain expertise, and organizational authority to run this integration, the synergy thesis is not executable.

**Red flags:** Synergy thesis that is entirely revenue-based with no cost component — revenue synergies are harder to achieve and take longer than cost synergies, and a thesis with no cost synergies may signal that the asset is not operationally adjacent; a strategic fit narrative that is generic enough to apply to any acquisition in the sector — "expands our platform," "deepens our data assets," "strengthens our competitive position" — without specificity; no named integration owner with clear authority and bandwidth.

---

### 5.2 Pattern's Operational Capability to Improve the Asset

**What to establish:** That Pattern has the specific operational capabilities required to execute the value creation plan — not just the financial resources to acquire the asset, but the people, processes, and institutional knowledge to make the asset better.

- Assess Pattern's track record with comparable acquisitions. Has Pattern successfully integrated an asset of similar size, business model, and complexity? What were the outcomes — did the value creation plan achieve its targets, on time, at the projected cost? If Pattern has not done this before at this scale, acknowledge it and assess the organizational learning and external support required.
- Evaluate the specific capabilities the value creation plan requires. For each initiative in the roadmap (section 3.2), identify the Pattern capability it depends on: a specific sales motion, a technology integration, a procurement relationship, an operational playbook. For each, assess whether that capability currently exists at Pattern in sufficient depth and whether the people responsible have the capacity to execute alongside existing responsibilities.
- Assess management team fit and retention risk. Does the acquired company's management team have the skills Pattern needs post-acquisition — and will they stay? A management team that is effective in an independent company may be ineffective inside a larger organization with different incentive structures, reporting lines, and decision-making processes. Assess the retention risk explicitly and structure the management equity package to address it.
- Identify what Pattern will need to build, buy, or hire to execute the integration successfully. Be specific: if Pattern needs a VP of Integration, a new technology platform, or an external consulting firm to run the 100-day plan, name it, cost it, and include it in the acquisition economics. Integration costs that are excluded from the model are not savings — they are deferrals that will compress returns.

**Red flags:** A value creation plan that requires Pattern to develop a new capability it does not currently possess, with no acquisition plan or hiring plan to build it; integration planned primarily by the acquired company's management team rather than Pattern — which means Pattern is not actually operating the asset, it is just providing capital; a 100-day plan that consists of "assess the business" rather than specific operational actions — this signals the integration thesis has not been thought through.

---

### 5.3 Competitive Advantage in the Deal

**What to establish:** Whether Pattern has a genuine advantage over other buyers in this process — and if Pattern paid a premium, what specifically justifies it.

- Identify Pattern's sources of advantage relative to the other buyers in this process: information advantage (Pattern knows this sector, this customer base, or this technology better than a generalist buyer and can underwrite risk that others cannot); synergy advantage (Pattern creates specific value through combination that a financial buyer cannot, which means Pattern can afford to pay more and still achieve the same return); relationship advantage (Pattern has a pre-existing relationship with management, customers, or the seller that reduces process friction and execution risk); or operational advantage (Pattern can execute the integration faster and at lower cost than a competitor).
- Assess whether the premium paid — above a comparable financial buyer's entry price — is justified by the synergy value Pattern creates. The standard test: if Pattern achieves all stated synergies, does the synergy-adjusted IRR justify the premium? If the synergy-adjusted IRR is below Pattern's hurdle rate, Pattern has overpaid for synergies that will not be realized at sufficient scale or speed.
- Evaluate what Pattern loses if it does not win this deal. If the asset is acquired by a competitor, does it materially damage Pattern's competitive position? If yes, the competitive harm avoided has value that should be included in the acquisition rationale — but only if the competitive threat is specific and proximate, not theoretical. If a competitor acquiring this asset is not material to Pattern's competitive position, the "defensive acquisition" rationale should not inflate the price Pattern is willing to pay.
- Assess the risk of overpaying in a competitive process. In a competitive auction, winning is a signal that you were the most optimistic buyer — not necessarily the best buyer. For each major assumption in the return model that differs from what a rational financial buyer would underwrite, identify the specific Pattern capability or synergy that justifies the more optimistic assumption.

**Red flags:** A competitive advantage narrative that is based on "we know this sector" without a specific information or synergy advantage that translates into a higher justified price; a premium paid that exceeds the present value of identified synergies — the excess premium is effectively a donation to the seller; defensive acquisition rationale that is used to justify a price above what the standalone return economics support without quantifying the specific competitive harm avoided.

---

### 5.4 Integration Plan and 100-Day Readiness

**What to establish:** That Pattern has a specific, executable plan for the first 100 days of ownership — and that the plan is sequenced to protect value before creating it.

- Map the first 100 days across three priorities in sequence. First, protect: what actions are required immediately to prevent value destruction — retain key employees (who, how, by when?), communicate with key customers (which customers, what message, by whom?), stabilize the technology infrastructure, and address any urgent operational or compliance issues identified in diligence? Second, diagnose: what does Pattern need to learn in the first 30–60 days that it could not learn in diligence — the actual state of customer relationships, the real operational bottlenecks, the cultural dynamics that will enable or resist change? Third, build: what value creation initiatives can be initiated in the first 100 days to demonstrate momentum and establish the operating cadence for the hold period?
- Assign ownership for every action in the 100-day plan. Not a workstream, not a committee — a named person who is accountable for the outcome. If a critical action does not have a named owner at the time of IC approval, it is not a plan.
- Identify the integration risks that are most likely to destroy value in the first 100 days: key employee departures, customer churn triggered by ownership change, technology integration failures, or cultural resistance to Pattern's operating model. For each risk, define the early warning signal and the mitigation action.
- Define what "Day 100 success" looks like in measurable terms. What KPIs should be on track by Day 100, what initiatives should be underway, and what early signals — positive or negative — would indicate that the thesis is developing as expected or that the plan needs to be revised?

**Red flags:** A 100-day plan that begins with "assess the current state" — diligence is for assessment; Day 1 is for action; a plan with workstreams but no named owners; a plan that does not address customer and employee retention as the first priority; integration cost and management bandwidth requirements that have not been included in the acquisition economics.

---

## Question 6: What Does the Seller Know That We Don't?

*The standard: a rigorous, adversarial assessment of the information asymmetry in this transaction — what the seller has observed over their hold period that is not fully reflected in the data room, what the process timing signals about the seller's motivation, and what conditions would cause Pattern to walk away. This gate is intentionally uncomfortable. Its purpose is to ensure that the investment committee has explicitly interrogated the most likely sources of adverse selection before approving the transaction.*

---

### 6.1 Seller Motivation and Process Timing

**What to establish:** Why this asset is for sale now, at this price, through this process — and whether the timing reveals information about the asset's trajectory that is not visible in the historical financials.

- Identify the seller's stated motivation and assess its credibility. The standard explanations — fund life cycle, portfolio rebalancing, founder liquidity — are not necessarily false, but they are also the explanations a seller would give regardless of the true motivation. For each stated motivation, ask: is this explanation consistent with the timing, the process structure, and the price expectations? Where it is inconsistent, investigate.
- Assess the process timing relative to the business's operating trajectory. Is the seller launching this process from a position of strength (recent KPI acceleration, a large contract win, a favorable market development) or from a position of deterioration (recent churn, a competitive threat emerging, a market development that will compress future growth)? Sellers who launch from strength have more options and less urgency; sellers who launch from a deteriorating position are more motivated to close. Identify which applies and assess what it implies about information not yet in the data room.
- Map the holding period against the business's development. If the seller is a PE sponsor, how long have they held the asset, and what has the value creation trajectory looked like? A sponsor selling after 3 years with strong KPIs and multiple value creation initiatives completed is a different signal than a sponsor selling after 7 years — past the rational hold period — with mixed operating results. The latter suggests the exit is driven by fund dynamics rather than asset readiness, which may mean the optimal value creation window has already passed.
- Assess whether the sale process structure itself contains information. A broad auction maximizes price but signals the seller does not have a preferred buyer — which may mean no strategic acquirer has pre-emptively approached them. A narrow process or a proprietary deal signals the seller has a preferred outcome, which creates a different negotiating dynamic. A process that has been restarted or that has unusual timeline pressure signals either prior process failure or a specific deadline — both of which have implications for Pattern's negotiating position.

**Red flags:** A seller whose stated motivation does not align with the process timing or price expectations; a business being sold at or near its peak trailing EBITDA with management projections showing deceleration — the seller is monetizing before the deceleration becomes visible to buyers; a process launched immediately after a large contract win, which may be using the win to mask underlying attrition in the rest of the customer base.

---

### 6.2 Information Asymmetry Assessment

**What to establish:** The specific areas where the seller's information advantage is greatest — and whether the diligence process has adequately closed the gap or whether residual asymmetry represents unpriced risk.

- Map the information asymmetry by domain. The seller knows: the real state of key customer relationships (not the reference accounts management provided, but the accounts that are quietly evaluating alternatives); the technology debt that has been deferred and will require investment post-close; the organizational dynamics — who is aligned with management's direction and who is not, who is a flight risk, and what the culture will look like once the sale removes the uncertainty that has been suppressing internal conflict; and the pipeline quality — which deals in the CRM are real and which are management's optimism.
- For each domain of significant asymmetry, assess whether diligence has adequately closed the gap. Customer interviews conducted by Pattern directly (not through management) close the customer relationship gap. Independent technology assessment closes the technology debt gap. Reference checks with former employees close part of the organizational gap. For each domain where the gap has not been adequately closed, quantify the potential impact on the return and assess whether a price chip, an escrow, a rep-and-warranty insurance policy, or a walk-away is the appropriate response.
- Assess the quality of the data room as a signal of information management. A data room that is exceptionally well-organized with comprehensive historical data is either a well-managed business or a well-prepared seller. Assess which applies: does the financial data in the data room match the narrative management presents? Are there periods or metrics that are conspicuously absent? Are the KPI definitions consistent across years, or have definitions changed in ways that improve the trend?
- Identify the questions that management has been reluctant to answer directly, has answered with unusual specificity (suggesting a prepared answer rather than a natural one), or has deferred to advisors rather than answering themselves. These are the highest-probability areas of information asymmetry. For each, assess whether the answer matters to the investment thesis and whether it has been adequately resolved through alternative diligence methods.

**Red flags:** A data room that does not include customer-level revenue data by cohort — this is the single most common omission that conceals churn dynamics; management who routes all customer reference calls through the company's advisor rather than making the management team directly available; financial KPI definitions that have changed in the 12–24 months immediately prior to the sale process, which may reflect retroactive optimization of how the metrics are presented.

---

### 6.3 Diligence Gap and Residual Risk Assessment

**What to establish:** The specific diligence items that remain open at the time of IC approval, the materiality of each to the investment thesis, and the explicit decision about whether to proceed with residual uncertainty or require resolution before proceeding.

- Enumerate every open diligence item at the time of IC consideration. For each: what is the question, what evidence would resolve it, why has it not been resolved, and what is the range of outcomes if the answer is adverse? Do not present open items as items that "will be resolved before close" — present them as items that are currently unresolved and require a committee decision about whether the residual uncertainty is acceptable.
- Assess the materiality of each open item to the return. Categorize open items as: thesis-critical (if the answer is adverse, the thesis does not work and the deal should not proceed); material but manageable (if the answer is adverse, the return is impaired but the deal may still be attractive at a lower price or with appropriate protections); and non-material (the answer does not change the investment decision). Only thesis-critical items should be IC-blocking at this stage.
- For each material open item, identify the contractual protection available: representations and warranties that cover the risk, escrow provisions that provide financial recourse, rep-and-warranty insurance that transfers the risk to a third party, or price adjustment mechanisms triggered by specific post-close events. Assess whether the available protections are adequate relative to the magnitude of the risk.
- Define the walk-away conditions explicitly. What specific finding — in ongoing diligence, in management reference checks, in customer interviews, or in the final contract — would cause Pattern to walk away from this transaction? Stating walk-away conditions before they arise is not pessimism; it is the discipline that prevents sunk-cost reasoning from driving a bad decision after significant advisory fees, management time, and relationship capital have been invested.

**Red flags:** An IC memo that characterizes all open diligence items as "standard" or "expected to resolve favorably" — this language suppresses legitimate uncertainty and prevents the committee from making an informed risk assessment; walk-away conditions that are not stated explicitly, which means the deal team has not committed to a line that would cause them to recommend against proceeding; rep-and-warranty insurance proposed as the primary risk mitigation for a thesis-critical diligence item — RWI transfers financial risk but does not resolve the underlying uncertainty about the asset's quality.

---

### 6.4 The Adverse Selection Test

**What to establish:** A direct, uncomfortable answer to the question: why is Pattern able to buy this asset, at this price, in this process? If the asset is as good as the thesis suggests, why hasn't a better-positioned buyer already acquired it, or why isn't the price higher?

- State explicitly the most likely adverse selection explanation for this opportunity existing. Common explanations include: the asset is too small for large-cap PE but too large for small-cap PE, creating a valuation gap that Pattern can exploit; the business model is misunderstood by generalist buyers and Pattern has a specific insight that allows more accurate underwriting; the seller has a preference for Pattern specifically (management fit, strategic vision, speed of execution) that is creating a proprietary advantage; or the asset has a specific characteristic (customer concentration, technology debt, management transition) that creates perceived risk that Pattern can underwrite differently than the market.
- Assess whether the explanation is credible and whether it is durable. If the opportunity exists because of a market inefficiency, how long will that inefficiency persist? If other buyers learn what Pattern knows, does the valuation gap close before or after Pattern can exit? If the opportunity exists because of a seller preference for Pattern, is that preference robust enough to hold through a competitive process if the seller's advisors push for a higher price?
- Identify the single most important assumption in the investment thesis — the assumption that, if wrong, most impairs the return. State it explicitly, label it as an assumption rather than a fact, and assess the probability that it is wrong. The investment committee should be making an explicit decision to accept the risk that this assumption is wrong — not discovering it post-close.
- Ask the adversarial question: if this investment fails to achieve its return target, what will the most likely post-mortem say was the cause? The answer to this question — stated honestly before the investment is made — is more valuable than any amount of upside scenario analysis. It is the single most important thing the investment committee should discuss before approving the transaction.

**Red flags:** An adverse selection explanation that is circular — "this opportunity exists because we are better investors" — rather than specific; an investment thesis where the single most important assumption is also the one that has been least rigorously tested in diligence; a deal team that cannot articulate a coherent post-mortem scenario — this suggests the downside has not been genuinely stress-tested.
